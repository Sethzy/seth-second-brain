#### Verbatim Text

one of the most helpful sales negotiation frameworks someone sent us during YC is the "isolation" method from Chris Orlob:

"at this stage of the negotiation, if we are getting price resistance, it’s usually because of one of these 3 reasons:

1) first, you may not yet see enough financial or business value in what we offer. If this is the case, asking for a lower price to protect your ROI is completely reasonable

2) number 2, maybe you are sold on the business case, but there is some sort of logistical constraint like a budget crunch or cash flow preventing you from moving forward

3) number 3, none of those are true and you are just being a good steward of your company’s capital by trying to get the best deal you possibly can

So which of those three buckets do you fall into?"

as soon as they answer the question, you start problem solving down each possible category

1 => doesn't understand the business value yet
2 => understands the business value, but can't afford the current structure
3 => none of the above, prospect is trying to get the best deal

always make sure to understand where your prospect is before you enter a negotiation

layer 1:
propsect: "we need to find a better way to manage our team's follow-ups. It’s a mess"

"answer: "okay, let's look at the math. if you had to estimate, how many of those "dark" leads should have moved to a second call last month?"

prospect: "probably 15 or 20"

always ask more questions. the goal is to understand the pain behind the surface answer

an investor shared these sales negotiation tips with us during YC which I thought were very helpful:

1) make sure you are the vendor of choice, always ask this first:

"I am happy to work toward a win-win agreement here, but it doesn’t make sense for us to start negotiating on price until you have chosen your vendor of choice regardless of price. So let me ask you this: if price were not an issue, would you choose to move forward with us?"

2) make sure price is the last hurdle:
"let’s say we agreed on price right now, what steps would you still need to take, or would your company still need to take, before we decided to move forward with a commercial agreement?"

3) find time to recap the business value:
"I would like to very shortly summarize the business value so that we know what’s at stake when it comes to coming to a win-win agreement."

4) define your “walk-away” bottom line before the call.

5) every "give" must have a "get":
If they ask for a discount, ask for a case study or a faster deal closing. If you give without getting, you’re "feeding the dragon" and they will only ask for more

1) don’t do this on day 1. you only use this with prospects where there is already active discussion, pricing has come up, and the deal is dragging. especially as an early-stage startup during YC, momentum matters and long cycles are not helpful

2) there is an difference between just offering a discount vs making it conditional on closing within a specific timeframe with the reasoning that this is more valaube to you than the flexibility you are offering

3) flexibility does not always mean a "discount". it can mean contract structure, payment terms, usage tiers as they scale, timing.... this is why it’s important to know how to run the follow-up meeting properly, which in my experience 9 out of 10 prospects will take

a lot of factors decide whether this closing strategy works or not. for example having a very strong well-positioned champion if you are doing enterprise deals who can carry this forward.

a lot of the "common" sales advice (starting high until someone complains, "just offering straight discounts" is a bad strategy, giving the "illusion" that you’re offering them a huge discount) sounds good in theory, but if you are actually scaling a company with the intention to grow fast, you need to be much more deliberate about how you run these calls.

5 common sales objections and how to overcome them:

1.  "I need to think about it" => 

"Totally fair. Help me understand, what specific part do you need to think over? Is it the price or the timing?"

"I'll think about it" is a polite "no". You need  to understand & isolate the real blocker before they hang up.

2.  "I need to run this by my boss/partner" =>

 "Makes sense. Would it be helpful if the three of us hopped on a quick call? That way I can answer their questions directly."

3. "I can't afford it" => 

"I understand. But help me out - when you say you can't afford it, does that mean you have zero budget, or just not for this specific solution?"

4. "We don't have the budget =>

 "I hear you. So if I'm understanding correctly, solving (Problem X) just isn't a priority for the company right now. Is that fair to say?"

Companies always find money for burning problems. "No budget" means "Not important." Call their bluff politely.

5. "I'm too busy right now." => 

"I appreciate that. It sounds like this just isn't a priority at the moment to fix (Problem X). Is that fair to say?"

If the problem is big enough, they will make time. If they agree it's not a priority, disqualify them and move on.

Step 7: Avoid offering long free trial and instead sign an annual contract immediately with a 30 or 60-day money-back guarantee (opt-out clause)

Step 8: aim for Gross Margin ~ 80-90%

Step 9: If your margin is low, raise the price or exit the deal (unless you care about market share).

Step 10 (and maybe most important: Don't try to match the lower price of competitors (it becomes a race to the bottom) and differentiate your product instead (functionality, specific integrations, …).

KEEP IT SIMPLE (complexity kills deals).
 Prioritize ARR/MRR over pure usage-based pricing to protect against downturns.

1) The viral wave has passed: Spring/summer hype (esp. Lovable’s June peak) pulled in lots of non-devs on monthly plans. They experimented and never returned. Cultural convo has also shifted from “AI can build an entire app” back to a more realistic view of AI’s limitations.

2) Power users eating up compute: A small group of power users burned through compute budgets. To protect margins, platforms throttled usage and raised prices. That scared away casual users.

3) Lovable’s need to monetize: After raising $200M, Lovable raced to monetize, tightening free credit limits and ending generous 50% discounts. The friction dulled its appeal for casual builders.

4) v0's pricing change: In May, v0 lost 500k (10%) of web traffic after moving from $20/month to usage-based credits. Casual users found their balance disappearing in hours, leading to outrage.

5) Replit’s missteps: Replit once thrived on its free tier and always-on Repls. But over the years, free education plans discontinued, always-on removed from basic plans. A high-profile AI agent failure occurred in July (accidentally deleting a live database) and user trust took a hit.

6) AI coding losing its novelty: AI coding is being absorbed into mainstream tools. GitHub Copilot, Cursor, and VS Code integrations keep developers in their IDEs, instead of web-first “vibe coding” sites. Google’s Firebase Studio also launched AI-powered app building with Gemini, eating into the traffic of these sites.

However, new entrants like Emergent have managed to keep growing despite bigger players losing traffic. 

It might be still in its hype phase - YC acceptance, fresh funding has helped it scale. The real test will be whether it avoids the same hype to drop-off curve.

Vercel’s v0 has also seen an increase in traffic likely due to its new Marketplace and deeper Vercel integrations. They also raised funding at the end of September, which could have resulted in an increase in web traffic. 

I wonder how players like Lovable, valued at a billion dollars, will maintain growth and valuation.
