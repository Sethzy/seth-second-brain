## Verbatim Text

Generative AI’s 6 Overlapping S-Curves: Infrastructure, Models, Agents, Apps, Consumer, Edge.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

AI does not resemble a single normal postwar technology S-curve. The historical record supports a more specific interpretation: AI is 6 overlapping S-curves compressed by pre-existing cloud, broadband, smartphone, software, semiconductor, and developer-distribution layers. Consumer AI trial and general-purpose usage have already moved faster than PCs and the early internet in several survey datasets, but enterprise production deployment, paid workflow penetration, and durable free cash flow are far earlier than the consumer usage curve implies. The most important analytical distinction is denominator discipline: “people who have tried a chatbot,” “workers using generative AI weekly,” “firms reporting any AI use,” “employees working at AI-using firms,” “paid enterprise production workloads,” and “gross-profit-positive workflow automation” are separate adoption curves. NBER survey work found 39.6% of U.S. adults age 18-64 used generative AI by August-November 2024 and 22.9% of employed respondents used it at work at least once in the prior week, while a Federal Reserve synthesis of 2025 surveys found approximately 18% firm-weighted AI adoption by year-end 2025, 78% employment-weighted exposure at firms using AI, and 41% work-related generative AI use among individuals in the labor force. Stanford’s 2026 AI Index separately reported 53% population adoption of generative AI within 3 years under its own methodology, while also noting large country-level variation. These are not interchangeable metrics; the divergence itself is the signal. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w32966/w32966.pdf))

The closest historical analogs for AI are semiconductors and Moore’s Law, cloud computing and SaaS, internet/broadband infrastructure with telecom overbuild, personal computers and productivity software, and the smartphone/app economy. Semiconductors are the best analog for performance-per-dollar compounding, bottleneck scarcity, cyclicality, and value migration from device volume to specialized components, design, manufacturing, equipment, and software ecosystems. Cloud/SaaS is the best analog for developer primitives, usage-based infrastructure, enterprise migration, hyperscaler oligopoly, and the lag between platform capex and application profit pools. Internet/broadband is the best analog for infrastructure leading applications, demand elasticity to lower latency and cost, and the risk that real demand can still coexist with late-cycle overbuild. PCs are the best analog for a general-purpose productivity platform where value accrued disproportionately to software standards and distribution. Smartphones are useful for app-store distribution and usage-intensity expansion, but less useful as a pure device analogy because AI can ride already-saturated devices rather than requiring a new household or handset replacement cycle. ([Newsroom](https://newsroom.intel.com/press-kit/moores-law))

The most misleading analogs are television, a generic “dot-com bubble” framing, IBM mainframes, smartphones viewed only as a hardware replacement cycle, and search/social media viewed as a single inevitable winner-take-most template. Television had a fast U.S. household adoption curve and powerful advertising economics, but it was a one-way consumer attention medium rather than a workflow-automation layer. Dot-com analogies are often too blunt because the internet was both a massive capital-destruction event and a foundation for the largest post-2000 equity winners. Mainframes show durable lock-in but overstate the likelihood of a single closed AI architecture because open-source/open-weight models, multi-cloud procurement, and enterprise bargaining power can limit model-layer take rate. Smartphone analogies overstate the need for new devices and understate the role of cloud inference. Search/social analogies overstate network effects for use cases where enterprise data, workflow ownership, trust, compliance, or integration matter more than global consumer graphs. ([TVB](https://www.tvb.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/National-TV-Household-Penetration-Trends.pdf))

The highest-conviction investment lesson is that adoption, monetization, profit, and market capitalization should be underwritten as separate curves. The postwar record repeatedly shows that infrastructure and enabling components often see demand before winning applications are obvious, but it also shows that late entrants into infrastructure capex can earn poor returns once scarcity normalizes. Scarce suppliers can outperform early when capacity, proprietary know-how, supply-chain control, or standards create bottleneck rents. Terminal value depends on whether scarcity persists after the supply response. The 2026 AI infrastructure boom has clear bottleneck characteristics: Synergy estimated cloud infrastructure services at a $500B annualized run rate in Q1 2026, up 35% YoY; Gartner forecast 2026 data-center systems spending of approximately $788B, up 55.8%; WSTS forecast the semiconductor market at approximately $1.51T in 2026, up 90%, with memory above $800B due to AI infrastructure and HBM demand; NVIDIA reported FY26 data-center revenue of $193.7B, up 68%, and Q1 FY27 data-center revenue of $75.2B, up 92%. These are extraordinary revenue and capex curves, but they are not proof of proportional application-layer free cash flow. ([CRN](https://www.crn.com/news/cloud/2026/cloud-market-share-q1-2026-aws-microsoft-google-battling-in-ai-era))

The central 2026-2030 investment question is not whether AI is “real.” The historical record indicates that transformative technologies can remain real while equity returns deteriorate due to overbuild, competition, price compression, regulation, or wrong-layer exposure. AI is underappreciated only where workload penetration is still low, production ROI is high, inference cost declines faster than demand elasticity, and revenue converts into gross profit after compute and implementation costs. AI is fairly priced where consensus already capitalizes a large share of future TAM into current earnings multiples without clear evidence of durable bottlenecks, pricing power, or free cash flow conversion. AI is vulnerable to overbuild where capex growth continues after utilization, backlog quality, pricing, or end-user revenue signals soften. The most useful leading indicators are production workload count, paid token/API volume net of price cuts, inference gross margin, data-center utilization, power/interconnect availability, renewal and expansion rates, measurable developer throughput, app-layer retention, and enterprise budget reallocation away from legacy labor or software spend.

WHAT WOULD CHANGE THE BASE CASE

The base case would become more bullish if 4 conditions emerge simultaneously: enterprise production workloads materially outpace pilots; inference cost per successful task falls faster than usage elasticity; AI-native or incumbent application vendors show durable attach, price uplift, retention, and gross margins after compute costs; and data-center utilization remains high despite 2026-2028 capex expansion. The base case would become more bearish if model capability plateaus despite higher compute spend, enterprise pilots fail to convert, open-weight models compress pricing faster than usage expands, hyperscaler/neocloud capex creates falling utilization, or chip/data-center depreciation accelerates because hardware and model generations obsolete faster than accounting lives.

METHODOLOGY AND DEFINITIONS

Technology S-curves are often discussed as if there is 1 curve. That framing is analytically inadequate. At least 7 curves must be separated.

1. Adoption S-curve: penetration of a defined population or installed base. The denominator can be households, individuals, firms, workers, devices, developers, workloads, subscriptions, transactions, or installed capacity. U.S. household TV penetration, U.S. adult smartphone ownership, global mobile subscribers, and enterprise cloud workloads are not comparable without denominator normalization.

2. Usage-intensity S-curve: frequency and depth of use after adoption. Examples include TV hours watched per household, internet minutes, mobile app sessions, streaming minutes, search queries, API calls, AI tokens, coding-agent tasks, or enterprise workflows executed. Usage intensity can keep expanding after household or individual adoption saturates.

3. Performance S-curve: cost per unit, performance per dollar, latency, bandwidth, density, energy efficiency, reliability, or quality. Semiconductors and broadband are the clearest postwar examples. The performance curve often begins before mass adoption and frequently creates the demand elasticity that later appears as consumer or enterprise penetration.

4. Revenue S-curve: conversion of usage into ARPU, subscription revenue, advertising yield, take rate, consumption billing, or maintenance revenue. Free consumer adoption can be a poor proxy for this curve.

5. Profit S-curve: conversion of revenue into gross profit, operating leverage, free cash flow, and durable ROIC. Streaming and AI infrastructure show why revenue and profit curves can diverge sharply when content, compute, or capex costs scale with usage.

6. Capital-cycle S-curve: capex acceleration, supply shortage, pricing power, financing boom, supply response, pricing normalization, depreciation, consolidation, and bankruptcy. Telecom fiber, semiconductors, cloud data centers, and AI accelerators are capital-cycle curves as much as adoption curves.

7. Ecosystem S-curve: emergence of complements, standards, distribution channels, developer communities, implementation partners, data flywheels, content libraries, app stores, and system integrators. Ecosystem development often determines whether value migrates to platforms and applications or remains in components and infrastructure.

Thresholds are most useful when applied to like-for-like denominators. A U.S. household adoption threshold for TV or PC should not be compared directly with global individual internet use, enterprise firm adoption, or cloud revenue. Historical threshold estimates below use 1%, 10%, 50%, and 80% where data support them. When exact annual observations are missing, thresholds are interpolated or labeled lower confidence. OWID’s U.S. household technology adoption dataset is useful for long-run household comparisons but is a compiled dataset from multiple sources and should be treated as a harmonized historical series rather than a primary census count. Census, Pew, Nielsen/TVB, ITU, GSMA, NBER, Federal Reserve, company filings, and industry-source datasets are used for denominator-specific validation. ([Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/technology-adoption-by-households-in-the-united-states))

Logistic and Bass diffusion models are useful only when observations are dense, denominators are stable, and saturation levels can be estimated. They are less reliable for enterprise AI because “AI adoption” includes heterogeneous use cases ranging from basic chat usage to embedded workflow automation. Threshold analysis is therefore more decision-useful than overfit diffusion models for AI in 2026. NBER technology-diffusion research and the traditional diffusion literature support S-shaped patterns for many technologies, but the investment problem is not merely fitting the adoption curve; it is determining which layer captures profit while the curve unfolds. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w27466/w27466.pdf))

Time zero is defined as the first commercially viable launch or first measurable adoption, depending on the curve. TV time zero is postwar commercial broadcast availability and household measurement around 1946-1950. PC time zero is mass-market microcomputer availability in the late 1970s to early 1980s, with Census household data beginning in 1984. Broadband time zero is measurable U.S. home broadband adoption around 2000 in Pew data. Cloud time zero is AWS S3/EC2 infrastructure launch in 2006. Generative AI time zero is ChatGPT’s launch in November 2022 for consumer adoption, but enterprise AI’s time zero should be measured from production deployment by workload, not from public chatbot launch. ([ICPSR](https://www.icpsr.umich.edu/web/ICPSR/studies/22720))

Historical TAM, revenue, capex, and profit comparisons should ideally be converted into real dollars using CPI or GDP deflators, but many source datasets report nominal dollars. This analysis labels nominal or forecasted values explicitly. Current 2026 Gartner, WSTS, Deloitte, and many industry estimates are forecasts or model estimates unless described as reported company results. Company-reported results, such as NVIDIA FY26 data-center revenue, Netflix 2025 revenue and operating margin, Salesforce FY26 revenue, or PayPal 2025 TPV, are realized company disclosures; forward-looking Gartner, WSTS, Deloitte, and EMARKETER values are forecasted or industry-estimated and should not be blended with realized history without an explicit label. ([NVIDIA Newsroom](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-announces-financial-results-for-fourth-quarter-and-fiscal-2026))

PLATFORM-SHIFT HYPOTHESIS SCORECARD

1. Infrastructure leads applications: historically supported. TV required stations, networks, and receiver manufacturing before advertising profit scaled. PCs required microprocessors, memory, storage, operating systems, retail/OEM channels, and enterprise support before productivity software standardized. The internet required browsers, ISPs, servers, fiber, routing, and payment/logistics infrastructure before search, e-commerce, social, and cloud profit pools matured. AI infrastructure demand is already visible in semiconductors, HBM, data centers, cloud, power, networking, and optics before application-layer profit pools are fully established. ([TVB](https://www.tvb.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/National-TV-Household-Penetration-Trends.pdf))

2. Applications often arrive 3-5 years after platform takeoff, but the lag varies sharply: partially supported. Consumer app layers can form quickly when distribution is frictionless, as Apple launched the App Store in 2008 with 500 apps and generated more than 10M downloads in the first weekend. Enterprise application profit pools usually take longer because procurement, integration, compliance, data quality, and change management delay production adoption. Cloud infrastructure launched in 2006, while the broad SaaS and cloud-native enterprise software profit pool compounded through the 2010s and 2020s. AI’s consumer distribution is faster than historical consumer platforms, but enterprise workflow conversion remains early. ([Apple](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2018/07/app-store-turns-10/))

3. Scarce bottleneck suppliers often outperform early, but terminal multiples depend on persistence: historically supported. Semiconductors, telecom equipment, cloud capacity, and AI accelerators all show early bottleneck rents. However, semiconductor cycles and telecom overbuild also show that extraordinary near-term revenue growth can be followed by inventory corrections, pricing normalization, and multiple compression. Cisco’s FY2001 charges included a $2.2B excess inventory charge after the dot-com/telecom boom, while the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco noted telecom companies increased capital spending by 206% between 1994 and 2000 before the bust. ([Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco](https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/er19-34bk.pdf))

4. Durable value migrates to workflow ownership, distribution, data, standards, and ecosystem control: historically supported. IBM System/360 created a compatible architecture and platform model; Microsoft Windows/Office captured PC software standards and distribution; Apple and Google controlled mobile app distribution; Google and Meta captured data-rich advertising distribution; Salesforce and SaaS vendors captured recurring workflow revenue. AI models alone may not capture durable economics if applications, data access, identity, workflow ownership, or distribution layers control the customer relationship. ([IBM](https://www.ibm.com/history/system-360))

5. Oligopoly outcomes are more likely when scale economies, standards, switching costs, data advantages, and capex barriers reinforce each other: historically supported. Mainframes, PC operating systems, mobile operating systems, search/social advertising, and cloud infrastructure all developed concentrated outcomes because standards, developer ecosystems, customer switching costs, scale economies, or data advantages reinforced each other. Cloud infrastructure remains highly concentrated, with Synergy reporting AWS, Microsoft, and Google at approximately 28%, 21%, and 14% share respectively in Q1 2026. ([IBM](https://www.ibm.com/history/system-360))

6. Commodity outcomes are more likely when standards are open, switching costs are low, supply becomes abundant, and differentiation is hard to sustain: historically supported. PC hardware, generic servers, DRAM/NAND cycles, parts of telecom equipment, and unmanaged hosting all show that volume growth does not ensure durable profit. AI layers most exposed to commoditization are model APIs without differentiated quality or data, generic neocloud capacity without utilization certainty, thin application wrappers, and infrastructure assets with high depreciation and low differentiation.

7. Enterprise adoption is slower than consumer trial adoption: historically supported. The contrast between NBER’s rapid individual generative AI adoption and the Federal Reserve’s lower firm-weighted AI adoption is the 2026 version of this pattern. Consumer trial requires minimal integration; enterprise production requires procurement, security review, workflow redesign, data governance, measurable ROI, reliability, and budget ownership. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w32966/w32966.pdf))

8. Large-cap incumbents can still produce alpha when investors underestimate a new curve’s impact on their earnings base: historically supported. Microsoft monetized PC standards and later cloud; Apple monetized smartphones and app distribution; Amazon converted retail scale into cloud infrastructure; NVIDIA converted GPU architecture into accelerated-computing dominance. The investment question is whether a new S-curve expands a large incumbent’s addressable earnings base faster than competitive intensity and capex burden erode returns. ([Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com/investor/reports/ar25/index.html))

9. Overbuild risk rises when capex growth continues after utilization, pricing, or end-demand signals soften: historically supported. Telecom fiber, PC inventories, semiconductors, streaming content budgets, cloud data centers, and AI accelerators all follow the same capital-cycle logic: scarcity produces pricing power; high returns attract capital; supply response arrives with a lag; utilization and pricing reveal whether demand was real at the required return on capital. The technology can remain strategically important while late-cycle capital earns poor returns.

MASTER TIMELINE SINCE 1945

1945-1955: Postwar electronics and TV commercialization. Broadcast television moved from experimental and prewar roots into mass U.S. household adoption after wartime production constraints ended. TV household penetration was 9.0% in 1950, 64.5% in 1955, and 87.1% in 1960 in Nielsen/TVB data. The commercial curve was driven by receiver manufacturing, station licensing, network programming, postwar income growth, and advertising-market formation. ([Census.gov](https://www.census.gov/about/history/stories/monthly/2023/september-2023.html))

1958-1969: Integrated circuits, mainframes, and platform computing. Robert Noyce’s monolithic integrated-circuit concept in 1959 and Moore’s 1965 observation created the performance/cost foundation for later computing curves. IBM System/360 launched in 1964 as a $5B, bet-the-company compatible mainframe architecture, replacing 5 existing IBM product lines and enabling software portability across models. The mainframe era created a platform, services, and switching-cost profit pool before the mass PC market existed. ([CHM](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/practical-monolithic-integrated-circuit-concept-patented/))

1970-1985: Minicomputers, microprocessors, relational databases, and early PCs. Departmental computing, DEC/VAX-style minicomputers, UNIX, early workstations, the first commercial SQL relational database, and hobbyist-to-business PCs moved computing from centralized mainframes toward decentralized users and departments. Oracle’s early relational database ran on DEC PDP-11 hardware, and Microsoft/Intel/IBM PC standardization ultimately redirected value from proprietary minicomputer systems toward x86 hardware, operating systems, and productivity software. ([CHM](https://www.computerhistory.org/timeline/computers/))

1984-2003: PC household adoption and productivity-software standardization. U.S. household computer ownership rose from 8.2% in 1984 to 15.0% in 1989, 36.6% in 1997, 51.0% in 2000, and 61.8% in 2003. Microsoft’s FY1996 report attributed growth to software license volume, with sales mix shifting toward licensing programs and integrated product suites. The PC curve demonstrates how hardware adoption can produce a larger, higher-margin software profit pool once standards and distribution consolidate. ([cps.ipums.org](https://cps.ipums.org/cps/resources/cpr/p23-208.pdf))

1994-2004: Web 1.0, ISP access, browsers, dot-com, and telecom overbuild. Household internet access reached 18.0% in 1997, 41.5% in 2000, 50.4% in 2001, and 54.7% in 2003 in Census data. Pew adult internet use reached approximately 52% in 2000 and 96% by 2025. The internet curve created search, e-commerce, online advertising, and cloud primitives, but the public-market curve included a severe bubble and telecom overbuild; telecom capital spending rose 206% from 1994 to 2000 before the bust. ([cps.ipums.org](https://cps.ipums.org/cps/resources/cpr/p23-208.pdf))

2000-2010: Broadband, Wi-Fi, mobile data, cloud primitives, and app stores. U.S. home broadband rose from 1% of adults in 2000 to 11% in 2002, 51% in 2007, and 80% in 2023 before measuring 78% in 2025. AWS launched S3 and EC2 in 2006, reducing the need for startups and enterprises to own physical infrastructure. Apple launched the App Store in 2008 with 500 apps, and apps moved from a novelty to a distribution layer for mobile software, ads, gaming, commerce, payments, and services. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/internet-broadband/))

2010-2022: Smartphone saturation, cloud/SaaS mainstreaming, streaming, social ads, and e-commerce scaling. U.S. smartphone ownership rose from 35% of adults in 2011 to 91% in 2025, while cellphone ownership reached 98%. Digital advertising reached $294.6B in U.S. revenue in 2025, with social at $117.7B and search at $114.2B. U.S. e-commerce reached 16.9% of adjusted retail sales in Q1 2026. Streaming reached 47.5% of U.S. TV usage in December 2025, while Netflix’s 2025 operating margin reached 29.5%, showing a profit curve that emerged only after years of content investment, subscriber scale, pricing, and ad-tier evolution. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/mobile/))

2022-2026: Generative AI, accelerated computing, and AI infrastructure. ChatGPT’s November 2022 launch made AI a mass consumer interface. By late 2024, NBER found 39.6% of U.S. adults age 18-64 had used generative AI, while OpenAI reported more than 900M weekly ChatGPT users, more than 50M subscribers, API throughput above 15B tokens per minute, and more than $2B monthly revenue by March 2026. AI infrastructure scaled at extraordinary speed: WSTS forecast the 2026 semiconductor market at approximately $1.51T, Gartner forecast 2026 data-center systems spending near $788B, and NVIDIA reported Q1 FY27 data-center revenue of $75.2B. These data indicate infrastructure and consumer usage are already large; enterprise application profit pools remain earlier and less proven. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w32966/w32966.pdf))

CURVE-BY-CURVE DEEP DIVES

1. TELEVISION AND BROADCAST MEDIA

Television is the cleanest postwar U.S. household adoption curve. The technical change was not a single invention in the postwar period, but the convergence of mass receiver manufacturing, broadcast spectrum allocation, station buildout, national network programming, advertising measurement, and consumer durable affordability. Wartime production constraints delayed mass adoption; after World War II, consumer electronics manufacturing resumed and TV ownership accelerated sharply. Nielsen/TVB data show U.S. TV household penetration at 9.0% in 1950, 64.5% in 1955, 87.1% in 1960, 92.6% in 1965, 95.3% in 1970, and above 97% by 1975. The 10% to 50% household curve took roughly 4 years; the 50% to 80% curve took roughly 4-5 years. Confidence is High for 1950-1970 observed penetration and Medium for the interpolated 1% threshold. ([TVB](https://www.tvb.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/10/National-TV-Household-Penetration-Trends.pdf))

Adoption accelerated because TV solved a high-frequency consumer use case: entertainment, news, sports, and shared cultural programming inside the home. Complementary infrastructure mattered. Stations had to reach markets, networks had to coordinate programming, advertisers had to fund content, and measurement systems had to translate attention into ad rates. Early TAM was likely framed as a receiver market plus broadcast advertising; realized TAM expanded into national advertising, sports rights, syndicated programming, cable carriage fees, local stations, color TV replacement cycles, and eventually multichannel bundles. The value-capture migration was from hardware scarcity to broadcast networks, local affiliates, content owners, sports leagues, cable distributors, and later streaming platforms.

The public-market lesson is that a fast adoption curve can make hardware winners look attractive early while long-duration profit accrues to distribution, content, advertising scale, and attention aggregation. Television also warns against confusing penetration saturation with profit-pool saturation. Household TV penetration was mature by the 1960s, but cable, color TV, national advertising, sports rights, and streaming reshuffled value for decades. The AI analogy is limited because TV was mostly a one-way consumer attention curve, not an enterprise productivity or workload-automation curve. It is useful only for understanding consumer habit formation and ad-funded distribution.

1. SEMICONDUCTORS, TRANSISTORS, INTEGRATED CIRCUITS, AND MOORE’S LAW

Semiconductors are the most important foundational postwar S-curve because they are a performance/cost curve before they are an adoption curve. The technical shift moved from discrete components to integrated circuits, then to microprocessors, memory, ASICs, GPUs, accelerators, advanced packaging, HBM, and chiplet architectures. Robert Noyce’s 1959 monolithic integrated-circuit concept and Gordon Moore’s 1965 observation that transistor density could compound at extraordinary rates created a self-reinforcing technical and economic roadmap. Intel describes Moore’s Law as the observation that transistors on an integrated circuit double approximately every 2 years with minimal cost increase, after Moore revised his original 1965 annual-doubling formulation in 1975. ([CHM](https://www.computerhistory.org/siliconengine/practical-monolithic-integrated-circuit-concept-patented/))

The semiconductor curve did not have a single end market. Defense and space supported early demand; mainframes, industrial electronics, calculators, consumer electronics, PCs, mobile phones, cloud data centers, and AI each created new volume and profit pools. This is critical for AI because the semiconductor curve expanded TAM repeatedly by enabling successor curves rather than saturating in 1 application. Value capture migrated across layers: merchant chip vendors, memory suppliers, Intel-style CPU architecture, Microsoft/Intel PC complements, Qualcomm wireless IP and basebands, ARM-based mobile ecosystems, TSMC foundry scale, ASML lithography, NVIDIA accelerated computing, HBM memory suppliers, EDA, packaging, and networking silicon.

Semiconductor markets also show that secular growth can coexist with cyclical capital destruction. WSTS forecasted the global semiconductor market at approximately $1.51T in 2026, up 90%, with memory above $800B driven by AI infrastructure, HBM, and accelerated computing; this is explicitly a forecast, not realized full-year history. NVIDIA’s realized FY26 data-center revenue of $193.7B and Q1 FY27 data-center revenue of $75.2B show the current AI accelerator revenue curve is already massive. Deloitte forecasted AI data-center capex of $400B-$450B in 2026 and possible $1T by 2028, with chips a large share of spending; these are industry forecasts and should be underwritten with supply-cycle skepticism. ([WSTS](https://www.wsts.org/76/Recent-News-Release))

The AI lesson is direct. AI infrastructure winners can be excellent early-cycle equities when bottlenecks are real, but terminal value depends on whether bottlenecks persist after supply arrives. Scarcity can migrate from GPUs to HBM, CoWoS/advanced packaging, networking, optics, power, cooling, memory bandwidth, data-center land, interconnects, or software orchestration. The investment error is extrapolating early scarcity margins indefinitely without modeling supply response, customer bargaining power, depreciation, and architecture substitution.

1. MAINFRAMES AND ENTERPRISE COMPUTING

Mainframes represent the original enterprise platform-lock-in curve. IBM System/360 was launched in 1964 as a $5B, bet-the-company move to replace multiple incompatible product lines with a compatible architecture. IBM states that System/360 separated software from hardware, allowed software written for 1 machine to run across the line, covered a 50-fold performance range, and generated more than 1,000 orders in the first month; by 1989, products based on the System/360 architecture and extensions accounted for more than half of IBM revenue. ([IBM](https://www.ibm.com/history/system-360))

The technical breakthrough was not just faster computing. It was architectural compatibility, scalability, peripheral ecosystems, software portability, and enterprise trust. Adoption began when large enterprises could justify centralized computing for banking, insurance, government, manufacturing, payroll, accounting, and transaction processing. The denominator was not households or individuals; it was enterprise workloads and IT budgets. Product-market fit emerged when mainframes automated high-value back-office workflows and created durable operational dependency.

Value capture accrued to IBM hardware, operating systems, peripherals, maintenance, services, and customer lock-in. The mainframe curve shows that workflow criticality and switching costs can dominate nominal hardware performance. It also shows that services and implementation ecosystems can be profit pools rather than low-value afterthoughts. The AI implication is that enterprise AI profit is more likely to accrue where AI becomes embedded into mission-critical workflows, systems of record, compliance processes, and proprietary data pipelines than where it remains a generic model endpoint.

1. MINICOMPUTERS, WORKSTATIONS, AND CLIENT-SERVER COMPUTING

Minicomputers and workstations were a decentralization curve inside enterprises and research institutions. They reduced dependence on centralized mainframes by making computing available to departments, engineers, laboratories, universities, and technical workgroups. Computer History Museum timeline material highlights Data General’s Nova minicomputer at $8,000 with 32KB of memory, reflecting a sharp affordability shift relative to mainframes. UNIX workstations and technical systems later supported CAD, engineering, research, graphics, and networked computing. ([CHM](https://www.computerhistory.org/timeline/computers/))

The acceleration mechanism was departmental autonomy. Users who could not obtain mainframe resources quickly enough adopted lower-cost systems for specific workloads. This created an enterprise-computing curve that was faster than mainframe procurement in some technical departments but still slower than consumer electronics adoption. Oracle’s early relational database and the broader relational database market were enabled by minicomputers and later client-server architectures; Oracle notes that early Oracle V2 ran on a DEC PDP-11 minicomputer, and the relational database became a system of record for large enterprises. ([Oracle](https://www.oracle.com/database/50-years-relational-database/))

Value migrated from proprietary hardware to operating systems, relational databases, networking, client-server applications, and eventually commodity x86 servers and Linux/cloud. DEC, Sun, and other workstation/minicomputer vendors were disrupted as PC economics, open systems, x86 performance, and cloud architectures absorbed workloads. The AI analogy is that specialized infrastructure can dominate early when it uniquely solves a workload, but open standards and commodity hardware can shift profit to software, data, and distribution once performance becomes “good enough.”

1. PERSONAL COMPUTERS AND PRODUCTIVITY SOFTWARE

The PC curve combined hardware affordability, microprocessor progress, operating-system standardization, productivity software, retail/OEM distribution, and enterprise desktop deployment. U.S. household computer ownership was 8.2% in 1984, 15.0% in 1989, 22.8% in 1993, 36.6% in 1997, 51.0% in 2000, and 61.8% in 2003. Census later reported that by 2015, 79% of households had a computer under CPS measurement, while 2016 ACS data showed 89% had a computer when smartphones were included. The denominator shift is material; desktop/laptop ownership and “any computer including smartphone” are different curves. ([cps.ipums.org](https://cps.ipums.org/cps/resources/cpr/p23-208.pdf))

Adoption accelerated when PCs became useful for spreadsheets, word processing, desktop publishing, education, games, home finance, and enterprise productivity. The realized TAM exceeded early hobbyist assumptions because the PC became a general-purpose work and communication platform. Microsoft’s FY1996 annual report described software license volume as the principal driver of revenue growth and noted a sales-mix shift toward licensing programs, upgrades, and integrated product suites. Office’s suite economics and Windows’ operating-system standardization turned a fragmented hardware market into a concentrated software profit pool. ([Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com/investor/reports/ar96/financials/md.htm))

Investors could have recognized the curve through falling hardware costs, expanding retail/OEM distribution, enterprise standardization, software attach, upgrade cycles, and emerging network effects around file formats and developer ecosystems. The AI lesson is that the highest-margin profit pool may emerge after the installed base standardizes and usage shifts from occasional experimentation to daily workflow. AI copilots and agents may follow a PC productivity pattern only if they become embedded into durable work artifacts, file systems, knowledge repositories, collaboration surfaces, and enterprise operating routines.

1. ENTERPRISE SOFTWARE AND DATABASES

Enterprise software is a revenue and profit S-curve more than a household adoption curve. The technical change was the movement from custom software and batch processing toward relational databases, packaged applications, ERP, CRM, developer tools, analytics, middleware, and later SaaS. The adoption denominator was not users but enterprise systems of record, business processes, seats, modules, workloads, and maintenance contracts. Relational databases became durable because they controlled mission-critical structured data and transaction integrity; ERP/CRM became durable because they mapped software to financial, supply-chain, sales, service, HR, procurement, and operational workflows.

Adoption started when packaged software became cheaper and lower-risk than fully custom builds. It accelerated when enterprises standardized processes, globalized operations, implemented client-server architectures, and accepted recurring maintenance or subscription economics. TAM expanded from automating existing accounting and inventory processes to managing customer relationships, supply chains, human capital, analytics, and compliance. Implementation partners and systems integrators became part of the ecosystem S-curve because enterprise software rarely delivered value without process redesign and data migration.

The AI implication is that enterprise application value is likely to accrue to vendors that control workflows, permissioned data, identities, approvals, audit trails, and systems of record. Generic AI features can raise demo quality without creating durable pricing. The decision-useful metrics are not prompt counts or pilot announcements; they are net revenue retention, module attach, workflow-level automation, gross margin after inference, renewal rates, measurable labor substitution, and customer willingness to pay.

1. INTERNET AND WEB 1.0

The internet/Web 1.0 curve began as a connectivity and publishing curve, then became a commerce, advertising, software-distribution, payments, and cloud-computing curve. U.S. household internet access was already 18.0% by 1997, reached 41.5% by 2000, 50.4% by 2001, and 54.7% by 2003. Pew data show U.S. adult internet use at approximately 52% in 2000 and 96% in 2025. The adoption curve was fast, but profit pools were not obvious in real time. Portals, ISPs, browsers, e-commerce companies, online brokers, web-hosting firms, and telecom equipment vendors all appeared plausible at different points. ([cps.ipums.org](https://cps.ipums.org/cps/resources/cpr/p23-208.pdf))

The adoption acceleration came from browsers, dial-up access, email, search, online news, e-commerce, auctions, web publishing, and eventually broadband. The realized TAM was far larger than early “online brochure” or “ISP subscription” thinking because the internet became a distribution layer for advertising, retail, software, marketplaces, media, cloud computing, payments, social networks, and enterprise collaboration. The expanded TAM included new search advertising, long-tail retail, user-generated content, cloud services, and app distribution.

The capital-cycle lesson is equally important. Telecom and fiber infrastructure was necessary, but late-cycle capital was destroyed. The Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco noted telecom capital spending rose 206% from 1994 to 2000. Cisco’s post-boom inventory charges show how real infrastructure demand can still be overestimated at the margin. The AI analogy is strong: the technology can be transformative and still vulnerable to capex overshoot if pricing, utilization, or end-user monetization cannot absorb the supply response. ([Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco](https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/er19-34bk.pdf))

1. BROADBAND AND NETWORKING

Broadband is a usage-intensity and ecosystem curve. Dial-up internet created access; broadband changed behavior by lowering latency, increasing session length, supporting streaming, enabling cloud applications, improving downloads, and making always-on connectivity normal. Pew data show U.S. home broadband adoption among adults at 1% in March 2000, 11% in May 2002, 51% in September 2007, 80% in 2023, and 78% in 2025, with survey-mode and wording caveats. The 1% to 10% curve took roughly 2 years, the 10% to 50% curve roughly 5 years, and the 50% to 80% curve roughly 16 years. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/internet-broadband/))

The demand loop was powerful. More bandwidth enabled richer applications; richer applications increased bandwidth demand; higher utilization justified network upgrades; improved access expanded the application TAM. Cisco, Juniper, carriers, cable operators, modem vendors, router suppliers, optical-equipment vendors, and later cloud/data-center networking suppliers participated at different layers. However, networking also demonstrates capex cyclicality. The early-2000s telecom bust shows that capacity constraints can flip into oversupply quickly when financing conditions and demand expectations outrun realized utilization.

The AI analogy is the bandwidth-demand loop applied to tokens, GPUs, HBM, networking, and power. Better models and lower inference costs can increase AI usage; increased usage can justify more compute; more compute can improve models. But the loop only creates durable equity value if the marginal unit of infrastructure earns an adequate return after utilization, price declines, and depreciation.

1. MOBILE PHONES AND WIRELESS NETWORKS

Mobile phones are a standards, spectrum, subscriber, and device-replacement curve. 1G created analog mobility; 2G digitized voice and text; 3G added mobile data; 4G enabled smartphone broadband and app usage; 5G improved capacity and latency but with less obvious incremental consumer monetization in many markets. GSMA’s 2026 Mobile Economy summary reported 8.8B wireless connections and 5.8B unique subscribers, around 70% of the global population. Pew reported 98% U.S. adult cellphone ownership and 91% smartphone ownership in 2025. ([GSMA Intelligence](https://www.gsmaintelligence.com/research/the-mobile-economy-2026))

Adoption accelerated because the use case was universal: communication anywhere. Value capture moved across carriers, handset OEMs, baseband suppliers, network equipment vendors, app stores, operating systems, advertising networks, and mobile commerce. Nokia and BlackBerry demonstrate that early leadership in one device paradigm did not guarantee control of the smartphone era. Qualcomm demonstrates the power of standards, IP, and modem expertise. Apple and Google demonstrate how operating systems, distribution, developer tools, and ecosystem control can capture economics after network access commoditizes.

The AI lesson is that standards and distribution can matter as much as raw technical capability. Model quality may be necessary but insufficient. Enterprise trust, developer adoption, APIs, data access, device integration, identity, app distribution, and procurement channels will determine where value accrues.

1. SMARTPHONES AND THE APP ECONOMY

The smartphone/app economy is one of the best analogs for AI distribution but a weaker analog for AI infrastructure. Apple launched the App Store on July 10, 2008 with 500 apps and reported more than 10M downloads in the first weekend. Apple reported that the App Store ecosystem facilitated more than $1.4T in developer billings and sales in 2025, according to a company-commissioned Analysis Group study; more than 90% of those billings and sales reportedly did not pay Apple commission. This is a gross ecosystem measure, not Apple’s App Store revenue, and it should not be treated as equivalent to profit pool. ([Apple](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2018/07/app-store-turns-10/))

The smartphone adoption curve in the U.S. was extremely fast once app ecosystems, mobile broadband, touchscreens, cameras, and carrier subsidies aligned. Pew data show U.S. smartphone ownership rising from 35% in 2011 to 91% in 2025. The app profit pool lagged the device curve. Early apps were games, utilities, and novelty software; later profit pools emerged in mobile advertising, rideshare, food delivery, mobile commerce, subscriptions, gaming, fintech, and creator/content distribution. Device adoption created option value before winning apps were knowable. ([Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/mobile/))

The AI analogy is strong for app distribution, developer ecosystems, and platform tolls. It is weaker for device penetration because AI is already available through existing PCs, browsers, smartphones, cloud APIs, and enterprise software. The key AI question is whether chat/agent interfaces become a new distribution layer that reorders app discovery and workflow execution, or whether incumbent software surfaces absorb AI features and preserve current distribution economics.

1. SEARCH, SOCIAL MEDIA, AND DIGITAL ADVERTISING

Search and social media are data, auction, and distribution curves. Search monetized intent; social monetized identity, graph, attention, and targeting; mobile extended inventory and usage intensity. U.S. internet advertising revenue reached $294.6B in 2025, up 13.9%, according to the IAB/PwC report. Social advertising was $117.7B, up 32.6%, and 40% of U.S. digital ad revenue; search was $114.2B, up 11.0%, and 38.8% of U.S. digital ad revenue. Digital video and commerce media added further TAM expansion. ([IAB](https://www.iab.com/insights/internet-advertising-revenue-report-full-year-2025/))

The realized TAM exceeded early banner-ad assumptions because auction pricing, performance measurement, conversion tracking, mobile targeting, creator inventory, retail media, and first-party data improved advertiser ROI. Value accrued to platforms with scaled queries, social graphs, identity, measurement, ad tools, and advertiser liquidity. Network effects and data advantages produced high concentration, but privacy regulation, platform policy, and AI-generated answer engines create new pressures.

The AI analogy is two-sided. AI may expand ad TAM through better targeting, creative generation, commerce assistance, and productivity tools for advertisers. It may also compress traditional search economics if answer engines reduce outbound clicks, shift query share, or change the commercial unit from keyword auctions to agentic commerce and paid recommendations. Incumbents with distribution and advertiser relationships may adapt, but the monetization transition could be margin-dilutive if AI answers cost more to serve than traditional search results.

1. E-COMMERCE AND PAYMENTS

E-commerce is a retail-replacement curve, marketplace curve, logistics curve, and payments curve. U.S. Census data show adjusted U.S. e-commerce sales of $326.7B in Q1 2026, up 2.7% sequentially and 9.8% YoY, representing 16.9% of total retail sales. This is a revenue-share curve, not a household adoption curve. E-commerce remains below 50% of retail because grocery, auto, fuel, restaurants, services, regulated goods, and many local categories remain partially offline or hybrid. ([Census.gov](https://www.census.gov/retail/ecommerce.html))

Value capture varied by model. Amazon captured scale through selection, fulfillment, marketplace fees, ads, Prime, logistics, and cloud adjacency. eBay captured auction/liquidity economics earlier but did not control fulfillment to the same degree. Shopify captured merchant enablement and software. PayPal and Stripe captured payments infrastructure, fraud/risk tooling, checkout, and developer integration. PayPal reported $1.79T of TPV and 439M active accounts in 2025; TPV is volume, not revenue or profit. ([Q4 Capital](https://s205.q4cdn.com/875401827/files/doc_financials/2025/q4/10-K.pdf))

The AI implication is that consumer agents may alter product discovery, comparison shopping, checkout, customer support, fraud, pricing, personalization, and merchant operations. However, the durable profit pools are likely to sit where trust, identity, payments, logistics, merchant relationships, and demand aggregation are controlled. AI can lower shopping friction, but it can also increase platform bargaining power and compress merchant margins if agents intermediate demand.

1. CLOUD COMPUTING AND SAAS

Cloud is the strongest direct analog for AI infrastructure and developer-tool adoption. AWS launched S3 in 2006 and EC2 a few months later, turning storage and compute into programmable services. AWS described S3 as scalable, reliable, low-latency storage available through an API, while EC2 offered resizable compute capacity designed to make web-scale computing easier for developers. This changed the startup and enterprise capital model: capex became opex, capacity became elastic, and developers gained primitives that previously required infrastructure ownership. ([Amazon Web Services, Inc.](https://aws.amazon.com/about-aws/our-origins/))

The cloud revenue curve is now very large and still accelerating due to AI. Synergy estimated Q1 2026 enterprise cloud infrastructure spending at a record $129B, up 35% YoY and 15x the level a decade earlier; Synergy also estimated the cloud infrastructure market at a roughly $500B annualized run rate, with AWS, Microsoft, and Google at 28%, 21%, and 14% share. Gartner forecast public-cloud end-user spending of $723.4B in 2025 and cloud infrastructure/platform services of approximately $301B. These sources measure different but related cloud spending definitions and should not be added together. ([CRN](https://www.crn.com/news/cloud/2026/cloud-market-share-q1-2026-aws-microsoft-google-battling-in-ai-era))

SaaS is the application and profit curve that rode cloud and broadband. Salesforce reported FY26 revenue of $41.5B, GAAP operating margin of 20.1%, non-GAAP operating margin of 34.1%, and free cash flow of $14.4B. In Q1 FY27, Salesforce reported Agentforce ARR of $1.2B, Data 360 and Agentforce ARR of nearly $3.4B, and 3.8B company-defined “agentic work units.” These AI metrics are useful for monitoring vendor-specific adoption but are not comparable to independent workload-penetration measures. ([Salesforce Investor Relations](https://investor.salesforce.com/news/news-details/2026/Salesforce-Delivers-Record-Fourth-Quarter-Fiscal-2026-Results/default.aspx))

The AI lesson is that infrastructure platforms can be very large and profitable when scale economies, customer trust, developer ecosystems, and breadth of services reinforce each other. The risk is also cloud-like: capex must be matched to durable utilization and pricing. Hyperscalers are structurally advantaged because they can amortize AI capex across training, inference, storage, networking, enterprise cloud, and internal workloads. Neoclouds and single-purpose capacity providers are more exposed to utilization, customer concentration, financing cost, and accelerated depreciation.

1. STREAMING MEDIA

Streaming demonstrates the divergence between adoption and profit. Broadband, mobile devices, connected TVs, codecs, recommendation systems, content delivery networks, and subscription billing enabled video and music streaming. Nielsen reported streaming at 47.5% of U.S. TV usage in December 2025, the largest share in the Gauge dataset, while Christmas Day 2025 exceeded 55B streaming minutes. This is usage share, not profit share. ([Nielsen](https://www.nielsen.com/news-center/2026/streaming-shatters-multiple-records-in-december-2025-with-47-5-of-tv-viewing-according-to-nielsens-the-gauge/))

Netflix shows the eventual profit curve after scale, pricing, international expansion, content library investment, password-sharing monetization, and ad-tier development. Netflix reported 2025 streaming revenue of $45.2B, operating income of $13.3B, and operating margin of 29.5%. Spotify reported Q4 2025 MAUs of 751M, premium subscribers of 290M, gross margin of 33.1%, and operating income of €701M. These models demonstrate that content/platform scale can become profitable, but only after years of investment and with very different margin structures from software. ([Securities and Exchange Commission](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1065280/000106528026000034/nflx-20251231.htm))

The AI lesson is that heavy usage can be economically ambiguous when variable costs are significant. Streaming minutes were not automatically profitable because content costs, churn, licensing, and competition absorbed value. AI tokens are analogous: token growth is bullish only if cost per successful task declines, pricing power persists, and gross profit expands faster than compute cost, support cost, and model depreciation.

1. CYBERSECURITY AND IDENTITY

Cybersecurity is a comparator curve because it is driven by platform shifts, threat expansion, regulatory pressure, identity complexity, and enterprise risk tolerance rather than discretionary consumer adoption. Each major platform transition created new attack surfaces: mainframes and terminals, PCs, LANs, internet, cloud, mobile, SaaS, identity, APIs, and now AI agents. Gartner forecast end-user information-security spending of $244B in 2026 and $322B by 2029, implying a 10% CAGR from 2024 to 2029; this is a forecast, not realized history. ([Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/en/documents/6998666))

The value-capture pattern differs from many technology curves. Security budgets are resilient because breach risk, compliance, insurance, identity, and operational continuity create non-discretionary demand. However, the market is fragmented because threat surfaces change and point products proliferate. Durable winners tend to control identity, endpoint telemetry, network visibility, cloud posture, data security, privileged access, or integrated platforms. AI will likely expand security TAM through agent governance, model security, data leakage prevention, identity controls, and automated security operations. The AI investment implication is that identity and security may be high-quality second-order beneficiaries even if application-layer AI economics remain uncertain.

1. AI AND ACCELERATED COMPUTING

AI is not 1 S-curve. It is a stack of at least 6 overlapping curves: infrastructure, foundation models, developer/coding agents, enterprise applications and agents, consumer AI/information access, and edge/on-device AI. The curves are compressed because AI rides existing distribution. PCs, smartphones, browsers, collaboration suites, cloud APIs, GPUs, broadband, and app stores already exist. This makes consumer trial faster than prior platforms but does not eliminate enterprise deployment friction. NBER’s late-2024 survey and the Federal Reserve’s 2025 synthesis show high individual usage but materially different firm-weighted and employment-weighted enterprise adoption readings. ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w32966/w32966.pdf))

AI infrastructure is the most mature revenue and capex curve. It includes GPUs/accelerators, HBM, advanced packaging, networking, optics, storage, power, cooling, cloud, neoclouds, data centers, land, grid interconnects, and software orchestration. Stanford reported that the U.S. hosts 5,427 AI data centers and that the leading AI-chip supply chain remains heavily dependent on TSMC. Deloitte forecasted 2026 AI data-center capex at $400B-$450B globally, with AI chips representing $250B-$300B, and highlighted rack power density rising from older data-center levels below 10 kW per rack to next-generation AI racks around 370 kW. These forecasts are directionally useful but should be treated as estimates, not audited demand. ([Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report))

Foundation models are in a quality-scaling, price-compression, and distribution phase. OpenAI reported more than 900M weekly ChatGPT users, more than 50M subscribers, more than $2B monthly revenue, enterprise revenue above 40% of total revenue, and more than 15B API tokens per minute as of March 2026. These are company-reported metrics from a private company and should be treated as management disclosures, not independently audited public filings. The model layer has scale advantages in compute, data, talent, distribution, and product iteration, but open-weight alternatives and customer multi-sourcing can compress pricing. ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/accelerating-the-next-phase-ai/))

Developer and coding agents are the most concrete application-adjacent AI use case because the workflow is digital, output is testable, ROI can be measured, and developers tolerate imperfect automation better than regulated operational workflows. OpenAI reported Codex above 5M weekly active users in June 2026, up more than 6x since its desktop app launch in February, with knowledge workers around 20% of users. Stanford’s 2026 AI Index reported major gains on coding benchmarks, but benchmark progress should not be equated with enterprise software output until release velocity, defect rates, maintenance backlog, security incidents, and engineering cost per shipped feature improve measurably. ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/codex-for-knowledge-work/))

Enterprise AI agents and applications remain early relative to infrastructure. McKinsey’s 2025 State of AI survey reported 88% of respondents saying their organizations use AI in at least 1 function, roughly 1/3 beginning to scale AI, 23% scaling agentic AI somewhere, and no more than 10% scaling agents in any function. The same survey emphasized that most companies had not embedded AI deeply enough into workflows to capture full value. This is consistent with prior enterprise S-curves: adoption language appears before system-of-record integration, budget reallocation, and free-cash-flow impact. ([McKinsey & Company](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai))

Consumer AI and information access are already mainstream in awareness and trial, but monetization is less mature. OpenAI’s scale metrics, Stanford’s population-adoption estimates, and NBER’s usage survey show broad demand. The unresolved issues are retention, willingness to pay, ad monetization, search substitution economics, copyright/regulatory risk, and whether AI assistants capture or redistribute attention. Edge/on-device AI is earlier. Privacy, latency, cost, and offline use cases support edge inference, but hardware replacement cycles, model compression, developer tooling, and killer applications remain less proven than cloud inference.

([CRN](https://www.crn.com/news/cloud/2026/cloud-market-share-q1-2026-aws-microsoft-google-battling-in-ai-era))

TABLE 3 — DENOMINATOR NORMALIZATION

Household denominator: best for TV, PC, home broadband, and home internet; weak for mobile, cloud, SaaS, and enterprise AI because household access does not measure usage intensity or workflow depth.

Individual denominator: useful for internet, mobile, smartphone, social media, streaming, and consumer AI; weak for enterprise ROI because an individual can be a casual user without paid production use.

Firm denominator: useful for enterprise technology adoption but biased toward small firms when unweighted; AI adoption can look low firm-weighted because small firms adopt slowly, while employment-weighted adoption can look high because large firms adopt earlier.

Employee or labor-force denominator: useful for generative AI work exposure, coding tools, collaboration software, and productivity tools; insufficient without measuring task share, time saved, quality, and output conversion.

Workload denominator: most decision-useful for enterprise AI, cloud, cybersecurity, databases, and automation; hardest to measure because firms do not disclose comparable workload units.

Seat denominator: useful for SaaS, productivity software, copilots, identity, and endpoint security; can overstate adoption when seats are provisioned but inactive.

Token/API/query denominator: useful for AI infrastructure and model providers; can mislead if price declines, inference cost, caching, failed tasks, or low-value usage inflate volume.

Revenue denominator: useful for cloud, SaaS, ads, e-commerce, payments, streaming, and semiconductors; insufficient without gross margin and capex intensity.

Capex denominator: critical for semiconductors, telecom, cloud, streaming content, and AI data centers; capex growth can signal real scarcity or late-cycle overbuild.

Installed-capacity denominator: useful for fiber, data centers, GPUs, HBM, fabs, power, and storage; decision-useful only when paired with utilization and pricing.

TAM AND VALUE-CAPTURE ANALYSIS

The historical record shows 7 forms of TAM that are often conflated. Ex ante TAM is what was knowable early. Realized TAM is what ultimately happened. Expanded TAM is new demand that did not previously exist. Replacement TAM is old spend displaced by the new curve. Profit TAM is the gross profit and operating profit pool. Market-cap TAM is public-market value created and destroyed. Time TAM or compute TAM measures human attention, labor hours, transactions, workloads, storage, network traffic, and energy demand captured by the technology. AI has unusually large potential across time TAM, labor TAM, and compute TAM, but that does not make every layer investable at any valuation.

Television expanded time TAM by centralizing household leisure and national advertising. Semiconductors expanded compute TAM by lowering the cost of logic, memory, and processing. Mainframes expanded enterprise productivity TAM by automating high-value administrative and transaction workloads. PCs expanded both household and enterprise productivity TAM. The internet expanded communication frequency, information retrieval, commerce, advertising, software distribution, and eventually cloud compute. Broadband expanded usage intensity by making online activity always-on. Smartphones expanded transaction frequency, location-aware services, photo/video creation, social engagement, and app-mediated daily behavior. Cloud expanded compute consumption by making experimentation and scaling elastic. Streaming expanded media time and shifted economics from ownership to access. Cybersecurity expanded risk-management TAM as attack surfaces grew. AI can expand software creation, knowledge work, customer support, search/information access, media creation, analytics, scientific discovery, and machine reasoning, but only some of those will convert into high-margin profit pools.

The most important AI TAM distinction is replacement versus expansion. AI infrastructure revenue can expand compute TAM, but enterprise buyers will eventually ask whether AI spend replaces labor, software seats, outsourcing, cloud waste, or customer-support cost. Application-layer AI is more valuable when it expands output or creates new workflows than when it merely shifts budget from one software vendor to another. Model providers are more valuable when they create new demand and maintain pricing power than when they become a pass-through cost inside applications. Hyperscalers are advantaged because AI can expand cloud consumption while also defending existing enterprise accounts. Application vendors are advantaged when AI attaches to systems of record and expands ARPU without proportionate inference cost.

MARKET STRUCTURE AND VALUE MIGRATION

Postwar S-curves show a recurring pattern: value begins at the scarce bottleneck, then migrates toward the layer that controls standards, distribution, workflows, data, or ecosystems. Television began with receiver scarcity and broadcast licenses, then value moved toward networks, content, advertisers, and cable distribution. Semiconductors began with device innovation, then value migrated across process leadership, design, architecture, equipment, foundry scale, memory, software, and end-market specialization. Mainframes concentrated value in IBM because compatibility, service, and switching costs reinforced each other. PCs commoditized hardware but concentrated value in Microsoft and Intel because operating-system standards and x86 architecture controlled complements. The internet destroyed early capital but later concentrated value in search, e-commerce, social, and cloud platforms. Smartphones transferred value from carriers and feature-phone OEMs toward Apple, Google, app stores, mobile ads, and mobile services. Cloud concentrated value in hyperscalers because scale, capex, services breadth, developer trust, and enterprise procurement reinforced each other.

AI’s likely value migration is already visible. The first wave accrued to accelerators, HBM, advanced packaging, networking, optics, data centers, cloud providers, and model labs. The second wave is moving toward developer tools, enterprise copilots, AI-enhanced SaaS, data platforms, identity/security, and vertical workflows. The third wave, if it emerges, will accrue to agents that control end-to-end outcomes: software development, customer support, legal review, finance operations, procurement, sales workflows, clinical documentation, analytics, security operations, and industrial automation. The key risk is that model providers absorb too much value through inference costs or platform control, preventing application gross margins from reaching software-like levels. The opposite risk for model providers is that open-weight models and model-routing tools commoditize access faster than usage expands.

The most attractive market structures for AI investors combine 5 attributes: bottleneck scarcity that cannot be quickly replicated; customer workflows with measurable ROI; proprietary or permissioned data; distribution through existing enterprise systems; and declining unit cost per successful task. The least attractive structures combine high capex, low switching costs, open standards, fast price compression, customer concentration, and limited differentiation.

CAPITAL-CYCLE AND OVERBUILD ANALYSIS

Capital cycles follow a consistent sequence. A technology breakthrough creates demand. Demand exposes a bottleneck. Bottleneck suppliers raise price or allocate scarce capacity. High margins and visible backlogs attract capital. Capital arrives with a lag. Supply expands. Utilization and pricing reveal whether demand was underbuilt or overbuilt. Late-cycle entrants with high financing costs, weak contracts, or obsolete assets earn poor returns.

Telecom fiber is the canonical case. The internet was real, bandwidth demand grew, and long-term data traffic expanded for decades. Yet late-1990s capital spending overshot near-term utilization and capital structures collapsed. The Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco’s 206% telecom capex increase from 1994 to 2000 is the relevant historical warning for AI data centers: real secular demand does not prevent interim capital destruction. ([Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco](https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/er19-34bk.pdf))

PC cycles show that adoption can continue while inventories correct. Semiconductors show that secular demand and cyclical pricing coexist. Streaming shows that usage share can grow while profit disappoints if content costs and competition rise faster than monetization. Cloud shows that massive capex can be rational when utilization is high, services attach is broad, and hyperscalers can monetize across multiple workloads. AI infrastructure currently has elements of both cloud rationality and telecom overbuild risk. The bullish interpretation is that AI inference will create a persistent, elastic demand curve that absorbs massive capacity. The bearish interpretation is that training clusters, speculative capacity, and neocloud financing are being built faster than profitable enterprise workloads can absorb.

AI overbuild should be judged by 8 signals: declining GPU rental pricing; rising inventory or order pushouts; data-center preleasing without creditworthy end customers; hyperscaler capex growing while cloud growth or AI revenue decelerates; inference price cuts that exceed cost declines; power/interconnect delays that raise cost per delivered MW; secondary-market weakness in accelerators; and accounting-life mismatch where chips or data centers become obsolete faster than depreciation schedules. The capex cycle becomes dangerous when capacity plans are justified by total-addressable-market narratives rather than contracted utilization, gross profit per workload, and customer ROI.

AI-SPECIFIC SYNTHESIS

AI infrastructure is in early-mainstream revenue takeoff but not yet through the capital-cycle test. Evidence includes NVIDIA’s data-center revenue scale, WSTS’s AI-driven semiconductor forecast, Gartner’s data-center systems forecast, and Synergy’s cloud infrastructure acceleration. The stage is not “early” for revenue or capex; it is early for proof of normalized ROIC after the supply response. The curve would move from early-mainstream to durable mainstream if data-center utilization remains high through 2027-2028, inference gross margins improve, and enterprise workloads absorb new capacity without price collapse. ([NVIDIA Newsroom](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-announces-financial-results-for-first-quarter-fiscal-2027))

Foundation models are in a scale, distribution, and price-discovery phase. Frontier labs benefit from talent, compute, data, brand, consumer reach, developer ecosystems, and enterprise trust. OpenAI’s March 2026 disclosure of more than 900M weekly ChatGPT users, more than 50M subscribers, more than $2B monthly revenue, more than 15B API tokens per minute, and enterprise revenue above 40% of total revenue indicates substantial commercial traction. However, management-disclosed private-company metrics do not answer model-layer gross margin, customer concentration, capex obligations, or competitive price compression. ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/accelerating-the-next-phase-ai/))

Developer and coding agents are the most decision-useful near-term AI application curve because the denominator can be measured: developers, repositories, pull requests, tests, release velocity, security vulnerabilities, build times, incident rates, and maintenance backlog. OpenAI’s June 2026 Codex disclosure of more than 5M weekly active users and rapid knowledge-worker growth indicates a broadening use case, but the investment signal is not users alone. The key metric is whether AI coding tools reduce total software cost per shipped, secure, maintained feature. ([OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/codex-for-knowledge-work/))

Enterprise AI applications are early but strategically important. McKinsey’s survey evidence of broad organizational AI use but limited scaling by function is consistent with the historical enterprise pattern. Early demos and pilots precede production because enterprises must solve permissioning, data quality, integration, compliance, risk, reliability, procurement, training, and workflow redesign. The stage moves from early adopter to mainstream when production contracts replace pilots, budget owners shift dollars from labor or legacy software, and vendors disclose AI-driven ARR, retention, price uplift, and gross margin after compute. ([McKinsey & Company](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai))

Consumer AI and information access are already in broad adoption but not necessarily mature monetization. The usage curve is fast because there is no device-install constraint. The profit curve depends on subscriptions, ads, commerce, app-store distribution, search substitution, and willingness to pay. Free or underpriced consumer value can be strategically powerful while still being economically incomplete.

Edge/on-device AI remains early. AI PCs and smartphones can reduce latency, preserve privacy, lower cloud inference cost, and support offline or embedded workflows. However, edge AI needs model compression, developer tooling, battery-efficient hardware, compelling local use cases, and enterprise manageability. The adoption stage is pre-inflection to early adopter for many use cases, except existing smartphone camera, voice, and embedded AI features that have already become normalized.

AI ADOPTION MEASUREMENT FRAMEWORK

Consumer awareness is a marketing metric. It is useful for brand and top-of-funnel distribution but weak for investment underwriting. Trial usage is more useful but still shallow. Monthly active use indicates habit formation, while daily active use indicates stronger utility. Paid subscribers demonstrate willingness to pay, but churn, ARPU, and usage intensity must be tracked. Token, query, and API volume indicate infrastructure demand but can mislead when price falls or low-value usage rises.

Enterprise pilots indicate curiosity and pipeline. Enterprise production workloads indicate adoption. Workflow penetration indicates economic transformation. Seat penetration can overstate value if users do not actively rely on the tool. Revenue penetration matters only if revenue is recurring and not offset by discounts, bundling, or cannibalization. Gross-profit contribution is the decisive metric for model and application companies. Free-cash-flow contribution is the decisive metric for equity value. Capex dependency is the decisive metric for infrastructure and model providers.

The most misleading AI metrics are free signups, downloads, demo counts, management anecdotes, “AI-enabled” product labels, token volume without margin, seats provisioned but inactive, pilots without conversion, backlog without credit quality, and capex announcements without utilization. The most decision-useful metrics are paid production workloads, gross profit per successful task, retention, expansion, budget substitution, usage frequency by workflow, measured productivity output, data-center utilization, inference cost decline, and model quality on real tasks rather than benchmark-only progress.

INVESTMENT FRAMEWORK

The practical PM framework is stage-specific. In invention and incubation, the best exposures are often venture-style and difficult to underwrite in public equities. Public-market investors should look for suppliers whose existing earnings are not yet capitalizing the new curve but whose assets are clearly advantaged. In product-market-fit stage, the best exposures are bottleneck suppliers, platform owners, and early application companies with measurable retention. In infrastructure-scarcity stage, the best exposures are scarce components, equipment, platforms, and capacity providers with pricing power and durable lead times. In platform-formation stage, the best exposures shift toward standards, distribution, developer ecosystems, identity, data, and workflow ownership. In application-profit stage, the best exposures are companies converting usage into recurring revenue, gross margin, and free cash flow. In maturity or overbuild stage, the best exposures are consolidators, low-cost survivors, software layers, and companies benefiting from lower input costs.

Picks-and-shovels include accelerators, HBM, advanced packaging, networking, optics, power equipment, cooling, EDA, semiconductor equipment, data-center construction, and grid infrastructure. Platforms include hyperscalers, major model providers, app stores, operating systems, data platforms, and enterprise software suites. Applications include coding tools, customer support automation, vertical AI, legal, finance, healthcare, sales, procurement, analytics, HR, and security operations. Distributors include search, browsers, operating systems, productivity suites, collaboration tools, app stores, cloud marketplaces, and enterprise sales channels. Second-order beneficiaries include cybersecurity, identity, data governance, observability, IT services, power, cooling, fiber, memory, and storage.

Valuation mistakes recur across S-curves. Early investors underestimate TAM because they frame new technologies as replacement markets rather than expanded markets. Mid-cycle investors overpay for bottleneck margins by capitalizing scarcity as permanent. Late-cycle investors confuse revenue growth with free cash flow and ignore capex, depreciation, inventory, or price normalization. Application investors overpay for usage without pricing power. Infrastructure investors underwrite demand but not utilization. Platform investors assume winner-take-all outcomes where open standards, regulation, or customer bargaining power limit take rate.

Earnings-call language should be parsed aggressively. Real adoption language includes “paid production workloads,” “renewal expansion,” “net revenue retention,” “measured ROI,” “gross margin after inference,” “customer budget reallocation,” “usage tied to contracted minimums,” “utilization,” “non-cancelable backlog,” and “workflow deployment.” Hype language includes “pilots,” “interest,” “engagement,” “pipeline,” “AI-enabled,” “transformational,” “early days,” “customer conversations,” “demos,” “tokens processed” without margin, and “large TAM” without conversion metrics.

Value is shifting from infrastructure to applications when 5 conditions appear: infrastructure pricing normalizes; model quality becomes good enough and widely available; application vendors show AI-driven ARR and gross margin; enterprise budgets move from pilots to production; and workflow owners demonstrate retention, pricing power, and data advantages. Value remains in infrastructure when capacity is constrained, utilization is high, customers prepay or commit, price declines are modest relative to cost declines, and bottlenecks keep migrating.

Late-cycle capital overshoot is detectable when capex growth remains high after backlog quality deteriorates, customers delay orders, GPU rental prices decline, utilization falls, financing terms tighten, inventory rises, or management narratives shift from customer demand to long-term TAM. AI infrastructure should be sized with explicit downside cases for lower utilization, faster depreciation, lower model prices, architecture shifts, and customer concentration.

Position sizing should separate strategic truth from expected return. A technology can be transformative and still be a poor long if valuation already discounts 5-10 years of flawless execution. Position sizes should be larger where evidence supports both expanding TAM and improving unit economics, and smaller where the thesis depends primarily on terminal TAM narratives. Long/short construction should pair scarce bottleneck beneficiaries against overbuilt or commoditizing layers, and pair workflow/data owners against thin wrappers or legacy vendors facing seat cannibalization. Scenario analysis should model upside from demand elasticity and downside from supply normalization, with explicit probability weights for infrastructure scarcity persistence, model price compression, and application gross-margin conversion.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR AI INVESTING IN 2026-2030

For 2026-2030, AI should be treated as a stack of investable curves rather than a monolithic theme. Infrastructure is already at scale and must be underwritten through capital-cycle discipline. Foundation models are commercially real but face uncertain long-term margin structure. Developer tools are the most measurable application wedge. Enterprise agents are the largest potential profit pool but remain early in production penetration. Consumer AI is a massive distribution and engagement curve with unresolved monetization. Edge AI is an option on future device and privacy/latency use cases.

The best historical setup for long exposure occurs when a curve is post-product-market-fit but pre-consensus TAM recognition, and when bottleneck scarcity or workflow ownership is visible in reported results. In AI, that setup exists selectively in infrastructure suppliers with persistent bottlenecks, cloud platforms with monetizable utilization, and software vendors that can show actual AI attach and retention. The setup is weaker where valuation already assumes permanent scarcity, where revenue is circular capex-funded demand, or where application usage is not converting into paid, gross-profit-positive workflows.

The best historical setup for short or hedge exposure occurs when late-cycle capital is extrapolated as secular demand, when competitors enter with undifferentiated capacity, when supply lead times collapse, when price cuts accelerate, or when revenue growth depends on customer financing rather than end-user ROI. AI short candidates are more likely to appear in overbuilt infrastructure, weakly differentiated capacity, thin application wrappers, legacy software facing AI-driven seat compression, and services models where AI lowers billable hours faster than vendors can reprice outcomes.

WHAT WOULD INVALIDATE THE BULLISH AI ANALOGY

The bullish AI analogy would be invalidated if AI fails the enterprise workload test. Broad usage without production workflow penetration would make AI look more like a consumer engagement tool than a general-purpose productivity platform. It would also be invalidated if inference economics do not improve enough to support daily workflow automation at attractive gross margins. If every useful AI task requires expensive frontier inference and customers resist paying for that cost, the application layer will remain margin-constrained.

The bullish analogy would also weaken if open-weight models close quality gaps enough to commoditize model APIs without allowing application vendors to keep the savings. That outcome would benefit customers but reduce returns at the model layer. A separate bearish path is capex overbuild: if data-center and accelerator supply outpace profitable demand, infrastructure equities could compress even while AI usage continues growing. This is the telecom/fiber lesson applied to AI.

The most severe invalidation would be a simultaneous combination of model-quality plateau, inference cost disappointment, weak enterprise conversion, and capex overbuild. The most bullish validation would be the opposite: continuing model improvement, rapid cost declines, high utilization, enterprise production conversion, application gross-margin expansion, and clear evidence that AI increases output rather than merely shifting software budgets.

FINAL INVESTMENT CONCLUSION

The historical record supports a constructive but selective AI framework. AI is not merely a normal S-curve and not merely a bubble. It is a compressed stack of infrastructure, model, developer, enterprise, consumer, and edge curves riding prior platform saturation. This compression explains why consumer adoption and infrastructure revenue appear unusually fast. It does not eliminate the historical lags between infrastructure buildout, platform formation, enterprise workflow penetration, and application free cash flow.

The most robust long-term AI equity returns are likely to accrue to layers that combine scarcity, scale, workflow ownership, data advantage, distribution, and falling unit cost. The highest risk-adjusted opportunities are not necessarily the fastest revenue growers; they are the companies where revenue growth converts into durable gross profit and free cash flow after capex, depreciation, inference cost, and competition. The highest-risk exposures are layers where valuation capitalizes permanent scarcity while supply is accelerating, or where usage metrics do not translate into paid production economics.

The base-case AI analogy for 2026-2030 is therefore semiconductors plus cloud plus PC productivity plus internet/broadband capital-cycle discipline. Infrastructure is real but must be monitored for overbuild. Models are strategically central but economically contested. Developer agents are the first high-signal application wedge. Enterprise applications are the largest profit-pool opportunity but require proof of production conversion. Consumer AI is already a massive distribution curve but still needs durable monetization. Edge AI is an option on future local inference and device refresh cycles. The investment committee should treat AI as early in workload penetration, mid-cycle in infrastructure revenue, late-early in consumer adoption, and unproven in broad application-layer profit capture.
