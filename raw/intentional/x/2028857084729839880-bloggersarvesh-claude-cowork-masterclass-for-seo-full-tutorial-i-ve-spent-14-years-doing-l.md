## 1. competitor review teardown

most business owners check their competitor's star rating and stop there. that tells you almost nothing.

what actually matters is review velocity - how fast they're getting new reviews compared to you. a business with 200 reviews that got 180 of them two years ago is weaker than a business with 90 reviews that gets 15 a month. google knows the difference. you should too.

this is the first thing i run for every new client because it tells me exactly where we stand and how long it's going to take to close the gap.

the prompt:

> "Open Chrome and go to these competitor GBP listings: [URL1], [URL2], [URL3]. For each competitor, read their last 50 reviews. Extract: total review count, average rating, how many reviews in the last 30/60/90 days, the most mentioned services in reviews, the most mentioned neighborhoods or cities, and any recurring complaints.

Then compare their review velocity to mine at [my GBP URL]. Output a spreadsheet with all this data and a separate tab that says exactly how many reviews per month I need to catch the top competitor and how long it will take."

why this matters:

the spreadsheet it spits out is basically your entire review strategy for the next 6-12 months. you'll know exactly how many reviews per month you need.

you'll see which services customers actually talk about (hint: it's usually not what the business owner thinks). and you'll find competitor weaknesses - recurring complaints are gold. if three competitors all have reviews complaining about communication, that's your angle.

this used to take me 3-4 hours per client. Claude Cowork does it in minutes.

## 2. service area page gap analysis

here's what separates businesses that rank from businesses that don't: landing pages.

if you're a roofer in dallas and you don't have a page for "roof repair in plano" and "roof replacement in frisco" and "storm damage repair in mckinney" - you're invisible in those cities. period.

your competitors know this. the ones ranking in multiple cities have built these pages. the question is: which pages are you missing?

the prompt:

> "Open Chrome and crawl my website [URL] and these competitor sites: [URL1], [URL2], [URL3]. List every service+location landing page each site has. Put it in a spreadsheet. Columns: service type, city/neighborhood, my site (yes/no), competitor 1 (yes/no), competitor 2 (yes/no), competitor 3 (yes/no). Highlight every combo where competitors have a page and I don't.

Then for my top 10 missing pages, open Google and search that keyword. Note who's ranking in the top 5 and what their page covers. I need to know what to build and what to beat."

why this matters:

this is where most local SEO agencies charge you $2,000 just for the audit. they manually crawl sites, build comparison spreadsheets, and research keywords one by one.

Claude Cowork does the entire thing and gives you a prioritized build list. not just "you're missing these pages" but "here's who's ranking for them and what their pages look like." now you know what to build AND what to beat.

i've seen businesses jump from ranking in 1 city to ranking in 8+ cities within 90 days just by filling these gaps systematically. it's not magic. it's just work that nobody wants to do manually.

## 3. GBP category audit

this one flies under the radar but it's massive.

your google business profile has a primary category and secondary categories. most business owners set these up once when they created their listing and never touched them again. meanwhile their competitors have added categories they didn't even know existed.

categories directly influence which searches you show up for in the map pack. wrong categories = invisible for high-intent searches.

the prompt:

> "Open Chrome and go to Google Maps. Search '[service] in [city]' for these 3 keywords: [keyword1], [keyword2], [keyword3]. For each search, note which of my competitors show up in the Map Pack. Then open each competitor's GBP listing and extract their primary category and all secondary categories. Put everything in a spreadsheet.

One tab per keyword. Columns: business name, primary category, secondary categories, star rating, review count, ranking position. Highlight any categories my competitors have that I'm missing from my GBP."

why this matters:

i've literally had clients add one secondary category and start showing up for a whole new set of searches the next week. it's one of the fastest wins in local SEO.

but here's what makes this prompt powerful - it doesn't just tell you what categories to add. it maps the relationship between categories and map pack rankings. you can see patterns. maybe every business ranking for "emergency plumber in [city]" has "water damage restoration" as a secondary category. that's not obvious. you'd never figure that out without doing this analysis.

this is the kind of insight that takes experience to interpret but takes zero experience to gather when Claude does the heavy lifting.

## 4. GBP photo audit

google has said publicly that businesses with photos get 42% more requests for directions and 35% more click-throughs to their websites.

but most businesses either have zero photos or uploaded 10 blurry phone pics three years ago and called it done. meanwhile the competitor dominating the map pack has 200+ photos, uploads weekly, and has before-and-after shots that make customers trust them before they even call.

the prompt:

> "Open Chrome and go to my GBP listing at [URL] and these competitors: [URL1], [URL2], [URL3]. For each listing, count: total photos, photos uploaded in the last 90 days, types of photos (team, jobs, before-after, office, trucks, equipment), and whether any look like stock photos. Put this in a spreadsheet comparing me vs each competitor.

Then give me a specific 8-week photo upload plan for my GBP. Tell me exactly how many photos per week and what type to shoot. Focus on before-afters, team on job sites, trucks in neighborhoods we serve, and close-ups of completed installs. No generic office photos."

why this matters:

photos are a trust signal AND a ranking signal. this prompt doesn't just audit where you stand - it gives you a complete 8-week execution plan.

the key insight: consistency beats volume. uploading 50 photos in one day then nothing for 6 months doesn't work. google wants to see fresh, regular uploads. that's what the weekly plan is for.

and the type of photos matters. before-and-afters show competence. team photos build trust. trucks in specific neighborhoods signal service areas to google. there's a strategy to this. the prompt builds it for you.

## 5. citation audit + NAP cleanup

NAP stands for name, address, phone number. it sounds boring. it is boring. it's also one of the most important ranking factors in local SEO.

if your business name is listed as "Smith's Plumbing" on google but "Smith Plumbing LLC" on yelp and "Smiths Plumbing" on BBB, that inconsistency hurts your rankings. google doesn't know which one is correct. so it trusts you less.

most businesses have 20-50+ directory listings across the web and at least half of them have some kind of error. old phone numbers. previous addresses. misspelled business names. every single one is diluting your authority.

the prompt:

"Open Chrome and search for my business '[business name]' + '[city]' across Google. Go through the first 10 pages of results and check every directory listing you find -Yelp, BBB, Angi, Yellow Pages, Manta, Hotfrog, Apple Maps, Bing Places, Facebook, and any others that come up.

For each listing log: directory name, listing URL, business name as shown, address as shown, phone number as shown, website URL as shown. Compare every listing against my correct info: [name], [address], [phone], [website]. Output a spreadsheet. Flag anything that doesn't match exactly, even small differences like 'St' vs 'Street' or an old phone number. Sort by highest authority directories first. That's my cleanup list."

why this matters:

citation cleanup is one of those things everyone knows they should do but nobody does because it's painfully tedious. you're literally going through dozens of directory sites one by one, comparing text character by character.

Claude Cowork just eliminated the tedious part. it gives you a prioritized cleanup list sorted by authority - so you fix Yelp and BBB before you worry about Hotfrog.

i've seen NAP cleanup alone move businesses up 2-3 spots in the map pack within 30 days. it's foundational. without clean citations, everything else you do works harder than it needs to.

## 6. backlink gap analysis

backlinks are still the single strongest off-page ranking factor. that hasn't changed.

but most local businesses have no idea what their backlink profile looks like compared to competitors. they don't know who's linking to their competitors. they don't know what opportunities they're missing.

this prompt fixes that.

the prompt:

"Open Chrome and go to Ahrefs. Run a backlink analysis on my domain [URL] and these 3 competitors: [URL1], [URL2], [URL3]. For each domain pull: total referring domains, domain rating, total backlinks that have DR 40+, and which pages get the most links.

Then find every site that links to at least 2 of my competitors but not me. Export everything into a spreadsheet. Tab 1: my backlink profile vs competitors side by side. Tab 2: sites linking to competitors but not me, sorted by domain authority."

why this matters:

tab 2 is where the money is. if a website links to two or three of your competitors but not you, there's a very high chance they'll link to you too. these are warm outreach targets. they already link to businesses like yours.

this turns link building from "cold emailing random websites and praying" into "reaching out to sites that already have a reason to link to you." conversion rates on this kind of outreach are 5-10x higher than generic link building.

without this data, you're guessing. with it, you have a hitlist.
