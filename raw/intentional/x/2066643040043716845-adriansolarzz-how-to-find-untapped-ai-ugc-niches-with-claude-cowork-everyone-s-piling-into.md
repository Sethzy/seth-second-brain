How to FIND Untapped AI UGC Niches With Claude Cowork

everyone's piling into the same 3 or 4 AI UGC niches, skincare, supplements, the usual.

and they're all fighting each other for the same eyeballs at the same time.

so what you'll do is start using Claude Cowork as a research engine to surface the niches that are wide open before everyone else gets there.

let me explain how:

---

## What "Untapped" Actually Means

first, get clear on what you're hunting for, because "untapped" gets thrown around loosely and most guys aim at the wrong thing.

an untapped AI UGC niche isn't just a niche with low competition. low competition often means low competition for a reason, no demand, no money, no way to convert. a dead niche is wide open because it's dead.

what you actually want is a niche with real demand and a real way to make money, where AI UGC specifically isn't being run yet. the demand already exists, the buyers already exist, and nobody's pointed a high-volume AI content engine at them yet. that gap, real money on one side, no AI UGC competition on the other, is the thing worth finding.

so every niche gets judged on 4 things at once:

demand. are people actively searching, buying, and engaging in this space already?

money. is there a product or offer with enough margin that content can profitably acquire customers?

visual fit. can the value be shown on camera in a short clip? AI UGC works hardest where the product or transformation is visible.

AI tolerance. will this audience accept AI-style content, or is it a niche where everything has to pass as perfectly human?

a niche that clears all 4 and has no AI UGC presence yet is the target. that's what Cowork is going to help you find at speed.

---

## Why Claude Cowork for This

now, you could do niche research by hand. people do, and it takes weeks and produces a shallow list.

Cowork changes the speed and the depth. it can run the research as an actual project, pulling and organizing signals across dozens of niches at once, holding all of it in context, and reasoning across the whole set instead of looking at one niche at a time. and with Fable 5 now powering it, the judgment layer doing the analysis is the strongest publicly available, which matters a lot when the entire job is judgment.

so what would be a multi-week manual slog becomes a structured research run you can do in a session or 2, going deep on every candidate instead of skimming.

the rest of this is the workflow you run inside it.

---

## Step 1: Map the Field Wide

so you start wide on purpose. the untapped niches are the ones that aren't top of mind, which means if you only brainstorm the niches you already know, you'll only find the crowded ones.

have Cowork generate a broad map of niches and sub-niches across the major buying categories, health, money, relationships, lifestyle, hobbies, B2B, and so on. then push it to go a level deeper on each, because the gold is almost never the broad category. it's the specific sub-niche underneath it.

the move is depth, not breadth at the top. "fitness" is crowded and obvious. the specific sub-niche of fitness for a specific audience with a specific problem is where the open lanes hide. so you have Cowork break every broad category into its sub-niches, and then break those into the specific audiences and problems inside them.

you want a long list at this stage, dozens of candidates, most of which won't survive the next steps. that's fine. the point is to start with a field wide enough that the open lanes are actually in it.

one thing that helps: tell Cowork explicitly to avoid the obvious. if you just ask for niches, it'll hand you the same crowded ones everyone already knows. ask it for the sub-niches and specific audiences that don't get talked about much, the overlooked corners of each category, and you skip straight past the saturated stuff that was never going to be your opening anyway. you're using the research to get past your own blind spots, so point it at the places you wouldn't have thought to look.

---

## Step 2: Score Each Niche on the 4 Signals

now the long list gets filtered. you have Cowork score every candidate against the 4 signals from earlier, and this is where the bad candidates fall away fast.

demand signals. is there active search volume, are there existing communities and audiences, are people already spending money to solve this problem. a niche with no demand is a dead end no matter how open it is.

money signals. what's the typical product or offer in this space, what's the price point, is there margin. a niche full of $5 products with no upsell can't support paid acquisition, so it stays small no matter how good the content is.

visual fit signals. can the core value be shown in 15 seconds. a skincare result is visible. a transformation is visible. a piece of abstract enterprise software is much harder to show, which doesn't kill it but changes how you'd produce for it.

AI tolerance signals. how skeptical is this audience, how much does the niche prize "realness," and is the content currently dominated by personality-driven human creators or by faceless informational content. faceless and informational niches tend to accept AI UGC easily. personality-cult niches resist it.

a quick way to read this: look at who the audience already follows. if the top accounts in a niche are faceless pages posting tips, facts, products, and aesthetic content, the audience doesn't care who's behind it, and AI UGC slots right in. if the top accounts are specific humans whose whole draw is them, their personality, their story, their face, then AI content fights an uphill battle because the audience is there for a person, not the information. the first kind of niche is the one you want, and there are far more of them than most guys realize.

Cowork scores each candidate across all 4 and ranks them. the niches that score high on demand and money but show little to no AI UGC presence rise to the top, and those are your real candidates.

---

## Step 3: Run the Competition Check

a niche can look open from the outside and be quietly saturated, so before you commit, you check who's actually running it.

have Cowork analyze the current content landscape for each top candidate. who's producing content here, are they humans or AI, what's their production volume, and how good is what they're putting out. you're looking for one specific situation: real demand being served by low-volume human creators or thin content, with no serious AI UGC operation in the space yet.

that situation is the opening. when an audience wants content, the existing creators can't produce at volume, and nobody's brought an AI engine to it yet, you can walk in and out-produce the entire niche from day 1.

the opposite signal, a niche already full of high-volume AI UGC, means you're late. it's not necessarily dead, but you'd be fighting for scraps instead of owning open ground, so it drops down the list.

---

## Step 4: Validate the Money Path

a niche with demand and no competition still isn't worth anything until you know exactly how the content turns into money. so you map the conversion path before you build.

have Cowork work through the question for each finalist: if you produce content in this niche, where does the money come from. is there a product to sell, an affiliate offer to promote, a service to book, a lead to capture and close. and what's the realistic value of a converted customer, because that number decides how hard you can afford to push.

a niche with a clear, high-value money path is worth far more than a niche with huge attention and no way to monetize it. attention you can't convert is a vanity metric, and plenty of "untapped" niches are untapped precisely because there's no clean way to make money in them. you want the ones where the path from view to revenue is short and obvious.

this is also where you decide the offer angle. sometimes the niche has an obvious product. sometimes the opening is that nobody's packaged the right offer for an audience that clearly wants one, and that's an even better spot, because you're not just first with AI UGC, you're first with the offer too.

---

## Step 5: Pressure-Test the Top Few

by now the list is short, a handful of niches that have demand, money, visual fit, AI tolerance, an open competitive lane, and a clear conversion path. before you pour production into one, you pressure-test it.

have Cowork play devil's advocate on each finalist. what's the reason this niche might be harder than it looks. maybe there's a platform restriction on the category, some niches get their content suppressed or ads banned. the audience might be smaller than the surface suggests. or the "low competition" is actually a sign that smarter people already tried this and left.

you want the holes found now, on paper, not 3 weeks into production. a niche that survives an honest pressure-test is one you can commit real volume to. a niche that falls apart under questioning just saved you a month of wasted output.

the ones still standing after this are your launch candidates.

---

## Step 6: Build the Production Brief for the Winner

you picked a niche. now Cowork turns the research into an actual production plan, so you go from "this niche is open" to "here's exactly what we produce" without losing momentum.

out of the research, Cowork builds the production brief. the audience comes first, who they are, what they want, what they're afraid of. then the angles, the hooks most likely to land with this specific audience. then the creator profile, what kind of AI creator fits the niche, the look and voice that reads as native to it. and the offer, what you're driving people toward and how.

this is the handoff from research into the production engine. the same brief feeds the concept generation, the scripts, the creator build with GPT Image 2, and the video routing from the cheap models up to the premium ones. you found the open niche, and now the machine produces against it at volume while the lane is still open.

speed matters here. an untapped niche is only untapped until someone else finds it, so the gap between "Cowork confirmed this niche is open" and "we're posting volume in it" should be days, not months. the research engine and the production engine running back to back is what lets you move that fast.

---

## The Openings This Tends to Surface

after you run this a few times, the open niches start falling into recognizable shapes, and knowing the shapes makes the research faster.

the adjacent niche. a crowded niche has a quiet neighbor. skincare is packed, but a specific skin concern for a specific demographic might be wide open. the demand spills over from the big niche, the competition hasn't followed yet.

the unsexy money niche. plenty of open niches are open because they're boring, not because they're broke. practical products, niche B2B tools, specific home or trade categories. nobody's racing to make content there because it isn't glamorous, but the buyers are real and the margin's often better than the glamorous niches.

the format-mismatch niche. an audience wants content in a format nobody's giving them. that information is sitting in long blog posts or dense forums, and nobody's turning it into short, visual content that's easy to scroll. demand is proven, the format gap is the opening.

the offer-gap niche. here the audience clearly wants something nobody's packaged for them yet. you're first with the content and first with the offer, which is the strongest position of all.

Cowork tends to surface these once you've told it what the shapes look like, and they're far more common than the "brand new niche nobody's ever heard of," which mostly doesn't exist.

---

## Why This Beats Chasing Trends

most guys find niches by watching what's already blowing up and copying it. by the time a niche is visibly blowing up, it's already crowded, and you're entering at the worst possible moment, late, against established players, fighting for whatever's left.

the research approach flips the timing. instead of entering a niche when it's obviously hot and crowded, you enter it while it's still quiet and open, before the trend is visible to the people copying trends. you take the open ground early and you're the established player by the time everyone else notices the niche exists.

and because Cowork can run this research continuously, you're never down to one bet. you keep a pipeline of validated open niches, you launch into them as you have capacity, and you're always working ground that the trend-chasers haven't reached yet.

the research compounds, every run teaches the engine more about what "open and profitable" looks like, so the niches it surfaces get sharper over time.
