## the size of the thing you're stepping into

affiliate marketing is not a side hustle anymore. grand view, statista and post affiliate pro all land in the same range: the global market is worth somewhere around $18.5 to $20 billion in 2026, the us alone is about $13 billion of that, and it's compounding at roughly 15% a year toward $71 billion by the early 2030s. over 80% of brands now run an affiliate program. average reported roi sits around 12 to 1.

two numbers matter more than the headline size. first, video now drives an estimated 55% of all affiliate traffic, which tells you exactly where the leverage is. second, the average affiliate reportedly earns around $8,000 a month, but 41% earn under $1,000. that gap is not talent. it's the system. the bottom 41% are posting by hand and hoping. the top are running production at volume.

## what affiliate marketing actually is

the model is old and it's clean.

a brand wants customers. instead of paying for ads up front, they pay you to send them customers. someone you send converts, you get paid. you don't own the product, you don't handle support, you don't touch fulfillment or refunds. you drive traffic that converts, you collect.

Glitchy is the network i use to find offers. it connects you to brands across apps, supplements, crypto, gaming, sweepstakes and fashion, and it's pure performance: the brand only pays on a real conversion, so you only earn when your traffic actually performs. nobody gets paid for nothing, which is also why the offers are real.

pick one offer. one category you can genuinely make content about. if you're new, start with sweepstakes or lead gen, the action is free for the user so the barrier to convert is almost zero.

## the traffic problem, and why short-form is the answer

every affiliate hits the same wall. how do you get people to click in the first place.

most people answer with paid ads. the problem is the economics turn on you fast. you need capital you don't have, you're bidding against companies with real budgets, and the second your creative fatigues the cpa climbs and you're spending more to make what you made three weeks ago. paid has a ceiling and you hit it sooner than you think.

organic short-form works the opposite way. a brand new account with zero followers can post one piece of content and get pushed to hundreds of thousands of people if the algorithm decides it's worth distributing. you don't buy reach. you earn it, and the marginal cost of the next post is basically zero.

tiktok's engagement rate sits around 3.7% in 2026, up nearly 50% year over year, while the platform is more saturated than ever. that combination is the whole opportunity: reach is still free, but only if your content actually earns the distribution.

## the formats that are converting right now

slideshows. tiktok's photo mode is not a trend anymore, it's a performance format. the swipe mechanic is the reason. because the viewer manually swipes each slide at their own pace, the platform reads active participation instead of passive watching, and swipeable carousels see roughly 2.5x the interaction of auto-advancing ones. analysts have clocked swipe content driving 15 to 20% higher click-through to the landing page, because someone who is already physically swiping is primed to tap. some measurements put carousel engagement as much as 80% above standard video. and they cost almost nothing to produce.

ai ugc. the second format works for one reason: it doesn't look like an ad. it looks like a real person recommending something they actually used, and that trust is what makes someone click your link. the algorithm treats native-feeling content as organic and distributes it instead of suppressing it.

the economics here flipped completely in the last year. a human ugc creator charges $80 to $200+ per video. arcads generates a realistic ai actor reading your script for around $11, in about two minutes, from a library of over a thousand actors. by early 2026 arcads had crossed 6,000 clients producing roughly 100,000 videos a month, and advertisers report engagement comparable to or better than human ugc. that's not a toy being tested. that's production infrastructure being run.

one honest note that protects your accounts: as of 2026, tiktok, meta and google all require ai-generated content to be disclosed. label it. disclosure doesn't hurt performance on native-feeling content, and undisclosed ai is exactly the kind of thing that gets a whole fleet of accounts flagged. keep it clean.

the rule under both formats is the same: volume is the strategy. one video flops. a hundred always find winners. you are not looking for the perfect piece, you're looking for the fastest path to a winner you can scale.

## the part where almost everyone quits

so the plan is obvious. find an offer, make slideshows and ai ugc, post at volume, let the winners pay for the losers.

then you try to actually do it by hand.

one person making content manually maxes out around three to five finished pieces a day before the day is gone. and that is nowhere near enough, because volume is the strategy and you physically cannot produce volume. you find a great offer, post a handful of videos, get 200 views, and quit by week two convinced the offer was the problem.

the offer was never the problem. you were the bottleneck. and bottlenecks always break, because they run on your hands and your hands run out.

this is the exact line that separates the $8k-a-month affiliate from the sub-$1k one. not the offer. not the niche. production capacity.

## what actually works and what doesn't

slideshows. highest roi format right now. cheapest to produce, strongest swipe signal, best for tutorials, listicles, before-and-afters, product breakdowns. test 10 to 20 variations a week, not one hero piece. this is your primary layer.

ai ugc. the trust layer. an ai actor that looks like a real person recommending the product. at $11 a video against $80 to $200 for a human, you can run angles you'd never afford to test otherwise. disclose it. use it to add a face to offers that need credibility.

faceless pages. lowest barrier, just content. the catch is geography. a lot of cheap faceless pages, especially on instagram, pull audiences from regions with low purchasing power, so you can get 500k views and almost no paying conversions. ios users in high-income markets convert several times better than the global android average. use faceless to test formats early, don't lean on it as your only distribution.

multi-account distribution. one account is one bet. several accounts running the proven format is several bets, plus resilience if one gets restricted. but this only works if every account is warmed up first. a warm-up is not following ten people and liking a few videos. it's three to four days of behaving like a real human inside your ideal customer's content, so the algorithm learns who your audience is before you ever post. accounts that can't clear about 1,000 views on their first few posts after a proper warm-up were seeded wrong, not given bad content. and creators on the same campaign should never interact with each other's accounts, it tells the algorithm the behavior is coordinated and it surfaces all of it to the same shrinking audience. keep it human, keep it high quality. the second it looks like spam, the platform treats it like spam.

bridge pages. non-negotiable. this is the difference between a content channel and a business. match the page headline to the exact hook in the video, one clear cta to the offer, no navigation, no clutter.
