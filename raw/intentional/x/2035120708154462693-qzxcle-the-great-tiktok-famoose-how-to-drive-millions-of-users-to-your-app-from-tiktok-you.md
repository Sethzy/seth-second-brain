## Verbatim Text

The Great TikTok Famoose

How to drive millions of users to your app from TikTok, YouTube, and Instagram?

---

Recollecting insights from my personal experience running TikTok GTM strategies.

## 1. Split the budget across three content types.

- TikTok-native KOLs with high engagement-to-follower ratios got 40% of the budget.

You need their creator instinct, trained and fine-tuned over years of experience. Things like deep niche pattern recognition, campaign selection instinct, a tested hook library. They *feel* what formats work, how to position the CTAs, why say X over Y, and so on. It's a compounding advantage emerging creators don't have yet. Read more on that here:

[Embedded Tweet: https://x.com/i/status/2032192567148781666]

- Micro UGCs took 50%. It's an incentive engine that crowdsources everyday TikTok users to create and push videos about anything. They run on a CPM-based system and can be considered safer than paid KOLs.

- And finally 10% of the budget went right to AI UGCs and slideshows. Experimental narrative marketing at virtually no production cost (in-house, fully controlled, takes a day to setup).

## 2. Numbers, numbers, numbers.

Track everything. Views, comments, likes, drop-off rates. Create custom CTA links and track CTRs with Google Analytics. It turned out that three paid KOLs did half the work. 1% of top performers drove 80% of total traction. The top guy pulled 107,500 views and 1,460 clicks. The second reached 154,900 views and 648 clicks. And the third pulled 95,500 views but only 64 clicks.

Which would be 1.36% CTR, 0.42% and 0.07% CTRs respectively. Same tier, completely different conversion behavior. I'll get to why in the next chapter.

Drop off rates is an especially interesting metric because it shows what's wrong with your videos. For example: graph #1 (bad hook), graph #2 (not interesting), graph #3 (no value), and graph #4 (banger video).

Micro UGCs followed the same pattern. The top three performers pulled 150,000, 65,400, and 50,500 views.

TikTok click-through rates are low. But it doesn't make sense to evaluate TikTok performance using metrics calibrated for Google or Meta. A 0.8% CTR with a 36% on-site conversion rate is a good rule of thumb.

AI gen content traction doesn't really differ from real people. Same for across slideshows and UGCs.

The most important metric tho is how many people share the video with their friends. Doesn't matter if it's AI or not. If people bring other people to your video, you win and close the distribution loop. The video becomes a marketing ouroboros.

## 3. Narrative.

The story was the differentiator between a 1.36% CTR and a 0.07% CTR. Not the creator. Not the production quality. Not the budget. Good storytelling can single-handedly make or break the whole campaign.

It is worth spending a lot of time trying to nail this part down. Engineer the storyline, manually handpick hooks and angles to lead the narrative.

Nobody on TikTok cares about your product specs or features. All top performing narratives answer one archetypal question: what's in it for me? Making money, better dating options, reducing health issues, or what? The niche matters less than the story. Find what your audience already cares about and fit the product into that world. Trading app? Easy wins and the lifestyle that follows. Fitness app? Jacked boys and girls pumping the weights and flexing muscles. Meal prep app? Merge the food and fitness angles. The story carries more weight on TikTok. Showcasing the product and hooks is just part of that.

And remember:

- if people watch but never download, your product is disassociated from the content. It's not part of it.

- if you convert well but don't get views, your product overwhelmed the content and it reads as an ad.

## 4. Normie crushed the influencer.

Content that focused on a regular person using the app to solve a specific relatable problem did better than anything that looked like a traditional promo post. The comments got filled with questions like "what is this" and "how can I use it" instead of slop replies or no replies at all.

This is the way to bypasses ad blindness. The KOLs are relatable, look and sound like the your own people. There's nothing plastic about them or their content.

Some of the proven formats:

- The "tutorial that is actually an ad" where you teach something valuable while the app is visible in the background doing the work.

- News hijacking, using trending headlines for virality.

- Fake lectures and guerrilla skits, hiring actors and creating "caught on camera" moments that feel organic.

- Aspirational POV formats like "POV: you are the girl who..." that position the app as an identity or a way to achieve that well desired lifestyle.

Find what is already going viral, then adapt that exact format to your thing.

And all that can be AI btw.

Note: Heavy promotional content that felt like shilling did not work. TikTok scrollers have a sharp filter for inauthenticity and they scroll past anything that reads like an *obvious* ad.

## 5. Geography targeting influences conversion.

I did not understand at first why everyone was so obsessed with targeting USA, Germany, and other tier-1 countries. Turned out these are the audiences who will pay if convinced.

Some GEOs produced low-quality traffic. South Asian audiences in particular showed high view counts but low downstream conversion. Choose GEO wisely.

## 6. Algorithm flooding works too.

Take media appearances and clips from the product team and distribute them through a network of clipper accounts at low CPM. The goal is to game the algo with volume. More content about the product on TikTok means relevance to the algorithm, which increases the organic reach of all other similar content. More views lead to more users, more content, more views.

You can derive a few content models with repeatable workflows and have UGCs, creator KOLs, or AI-generated content pick them up and scale.

# What I Would Do Now.

I think AI UGC is the future tho.

Slideshows, faceless content, talking heads. These were tested as scalable templates that can be produced in volume without loads of budget and ay time commitment. The cost of experimental marketing is now 10x less. AI creator networks can be built to experiment with any content format.

Seed the narrative. Identify winning formats. Then have paid KOLs replicate whatever landed well with high-quality videos. It's that simple.

The mainstream approach is mostly automating and optimizing AI x Content for production capacity. However, creative work and quality of the outcome still depends on manual human effort. Automation and content generation have mostly been solved. It is a volume game now. But properly setting up and steering the system in the right direction is the hardest part. Nevertheless, fully automating the whole marketing pipeline and expecting high ROIs is unreal as for now. This new AI UGC content automation looks easy on paper.

Setting up a phone farm is the hardest part. I burned through a bunch of accounts to figure it out. Read more on the importance of the seeding, faking your GEO, and scaling to hundreds of videos a day:

[Embedded Tweet: https://x.com/i/status/2033572145515712594]

Scaling does look too difficult if you do the math. About 20M+ views with AI UGCs is simply:

- $200 a day in API credits (Claude, Nanobanana, Kling, etc)

- 120 aged accounts

- 2 to 3 videos a day

- 1k to 5k views per video.

By the time you get to week 2 you will already stacking hundreds of thousands of views per day. Because it is a volume game. And then some 5 - 7 odd ones blow up with 700k to 3M views and take you to the next level.
