Workflow #1: Competitive Intelligence That Actually Delivers

Most competitive research is garbage.

Someone searches "[competitor name] features," skims three articles, copies bullet points into a slide deck, and calls it analysis.

Here's what we do now.

I give Opus 4.6 a list of 5-7 competitors and a specific question: "How are these companies positioning their enterprise pricing, and what objections are they preemptively addressing in their sales messaging?"

The model doesn't just search. It hunts.

According to Anthropic's benchmarks, Opus 4.6 dominates BrowseComp with 84.0% a 16.2 percentage point improvement over Opus 4.5. That translates to systematic, multi-step research that follows trails across pricing pages, case studies, LinkedIn posts from executives, and investor presentations.

In one session on February 6th, I asked it to analyze how three SaaS competitors were messaging AI features to enterprise buyers.

What it found:

- Company A buried AI capabilities in footnotes but emphasized "human oversight" in headlines (targeting risk-averse buyers)

- Company B showcased AI prominently but only in use cases for junior roles (avoiding executive displacement fears)

- Company C positioned AI as "copilot" in 14 of 16 landing page instances (specific language pattern to reduce automation anxiety)

That level of pattern recognition across multiple sources? That's what the 1M token context window enables.

The process: I created a folder with competitor URLs, brand positioning docs, and our current messaging. I asked Opus 4.6 to identify gaps and differentiation opportunities.

Output: 6-page competitive intelligence brief with specific messaging recommendations, quote-mined directly from competitor materials, delivered in 38 minutes.

Our strategist spent 20 minutes reviewing and adding client-specific context.

Total time: 58 minutes.

Previous process: 6 hours across two people.

Time saved per competitive analysis: 5 hours.

Workflow #2: Campaign Briefs That Don't Suck

Campaign briefs are where marketing projects go to die.

You need: audience research, messaging frameworks, channel strategy, content calendars, success metrics, and creative direction—all aligned.

Most teams produce a 3-page Word doc with vague language and no actionable detail.

We now produce production-ready campaign briefs in under an hour.

Here's how.

I feed Opus 4.6 our: client brand guidelines (12 pages), previous campaign performance data (spreadsheet), target audience research (surveys + analytics), and a 2-paragraph objective.

The model's adaptive thinking feature kicks in. According to Anthropic, this allows Claude to "dynamically decide when and how much reasoning is required." On simple tasks, it moves fast. On complex strategic questions, it allocates more cognitive effort.

Campaign planning is complex. The model knows this.

What it generates:

- Audience segmentation with specific pain points mapped to messaging angles

- 8-week content calendar with asset types, channels, and KPIs per piece

- Creative direction with specific visual references and brand voice examples

- Success metrics with benchmark data from similar campaigns (pulled from research)

The first time we ran this, our VP of Strategy reviewed the output and said: "This would've taken me a full day. Where did it pull the benchmark data?"

The answer: Opus 4.6 cross-referenced our historical campaign data (in the spreadsheet), synthesized performance patterns, and built projections.

The workflow: I created a project folder with all source materials. One prompt: "Build a comprehensive campaign brief for [client] targeting [audience] with [objective], using our historical performance data and brand guidelines."

Output delivered in 42 minutes, including a full competitive landscape analysis I didn't request but that added genuine strategic value.

Our strategist spent 25 minutes refining messaging and adding client-specific nuances.

Total time: 67 minutes.

Previous process: 8-12 hours across strategy and creative teams.

Time saved per campaign brief: 7-10 hours.

Monthly client reports are soul-crushing.

You're pulling data from Google Analytics, Meta Ads Manager, LinkedIn Campaign Manager, and your CRM. You're building charts. You're writing explanations. You're formatting slides.

It takes 4-6 hours per client. Nobody enjoys it.

Opus 4.6 changed this completely.

One prompt: "Analyze performance across all channels, identify wins and underperformance, explain why based on campaign changes, and create a presentation-ready report with visualizations and strategic recommendations."

What happens next is remarkable.

Opus 4.6 processes the entire data set (remember: 1M token context window in beta). It identifies patterns across channels. It compares month-over-month and against stated goals. It generates explanations grounded in actual campaign changes.

Example output from last week:

However, conversion rate dropped 8% because landing page copy still used feature-first framing. Recommendation: Align landing page to match ad messaging or revert ads to feature-first and test separately."

That's not generic AI writing. That's analysis that required connecting multiple data points and understanding causation.

It created the slides. Charts included. Formatted to our brand guidelines.

Our account manager spent 30 minutes reviewing data accuracy and adding client-specific color commentary.

- Zero instances of generic marketing jargon (because we trained it what to avoid)

- Natural integration of industry terminology and client-specific language

- Consistent voice across different writers using the system

Workflow #5: Ad Copy Testing (The System That Tripled Our CTR)

Most ad copy optimization is guessing.

You write 3-4 variations. You test them. Some perform better. You have no idea why.

Opus 4.6 systematized this.

Here's the approach: I gave it our last 6 months of ad performance data—campaign objectives, ad copy variations, CTR and conversion rates, audience targeting, and winning vs. losing patterns.

One prompt: "Analyze what makes our high-performing ad copy work, identify the pattern differences from underperformers, and generate 15 new variations applying these principles to [new campaign objective]."

The model's performance on GDPval-AA (economically valuable knowledge work) is 1,606 Elo 190 points higher than Opus 4.5 and 144 points above GPT-5.2. That reasoning capability shows up in how it analyzes marketing data.

What it found:

Our highest-performing ads (CTR >4.2%) shared three attributes:

1. Led with specific numbers or timeframes

2. Used second-person questions in headlines

3. Ended with action verbs, not adjectives

Our lowest-performing ads (CTR <1.8%) shared two attributes:

1. Led with generic value propositions

2. Used passive voice in CTAs

It generated 15 variations applying the winning pattern.

Opus 4.6 variations averaged 3.8% CTR. Human variations averaged 2.4% CTR. Best Opus variation hit 5.1% CTR.

This isn't replacing creative judgment. It's applying pattern recognition at scale that humans can't match.

Our copywriter now uses Opus 4.6 to generate data-driven variations, then applies creative intuition to refine the top performers.

Time saved per ad campaign: 2-3 hours in initial copy creation. Projecting 4-6 additional hours saved in testing cycles if early CTR trends hold.
