## Verbatim Text

Stop making marketing prompts.  
Start designing marketing loops.

A prompt gets you one answer.

A loop gives you a system that keeps working after you close the laptop.

This is the shift happening in AI right now. Boris Cherny, who leads Claude Code at Anthropic, has been quoted saying he doesn’t really “prompt Claude” anymore. He writes loops that prompt Claude, inspect the results, and decide what to do next.

That same idea applies directly to digital marketing.

Most agencies are still using AI like this:

“Write me 10 ad headlines.”  
“Summarize this call.”  
“Give me campaign ideas.”  
“Rewrite this landing page.”

Useful? Yes.

Leverage? Limited.

The real leverage is when the system runs every day without waiting for you.

A digital marketing loop looks like this:

Every morning, pull yesterday’s ad performance.  
Compare it against the campaign goal.  
Flag weak ads, rising costs, broken tracking, bad lead quality, and landing-page drop-off.  
Write the findings to a shared state file.  
Recommend the next action.  
Wait for human approval before spend, publishing, or client-facing changes.

That is not a prompt.  
That is an operating system.

The best marketing teams will not just have “AI prompts.”

They will have loops for:

- creative fatigue detection
- ad variation generation
- landing-page QA
- lead-routing checks
- CRM follow-up audits
- weekly client briefs
- competitor monitoring
- offer testing
- campaign anomaly detection
- sales/marketing handoff gaps

The basic pattern is simple:

1. Trigger  
Something fires without you: schedule, webhook, new lead, ad spend change, form submission, failed tracking event.

2. Memory  
The loop reads a durable file or database: what happened last run, what is in progress, what is blocked, what needs review.

3. Work  
The agent audits, drafts, compares, checks, or recommends.

4. Verification  
The loop checks something objective: campaign data, CRM records, page status, form submission, conversion event, build result, QA checklist.

5. Human gate  
The loop can recommend freely, but cannot publish, launch, increase spend, send client messages, or make irreversible changes without approval.

6. Update  
The loop writes back what changed and what should happen next.

That is the marketing version of loop engineering.

Not “write me better copy.”

More like:

“Every day at 7am, review all active Meta campaigns, identify creative fatigue, compare lead quality against CRM status, draft three replacement ad angles, and send me only the campaigns that need action.”

Or:

“When a new lead comes in, check source, form answers, routing, speed-to-lead, opportunity creation, and whether the right human was notified. If anything breaks, flag it.”

Or:

“Every Friday, generate a client-ready growth brief that explains what changed, what we learned, what we recommend, and what we are not touching yet.”

That is where AI becomes useful in digital marketing.

Not as a copywriter in a chat box.

As a layer of always-on inspection, triage, drafting, and QA.

The marketer’s job changes.

You stop asking for one-off outputs.  
You start designing the system that produces, checks, and escalates the right outputs automatically.

Prompts are tasks.  
Loops are leverage.
