## Verbatim Text

AI reduces the cost of producing work, which increases the amount of work companies attempt. The labor market impact depends on whether the demand expansion is larger than the labor-saving effect. In software, sales, marketing, security, analytics, and operations, AI may create a project explosion before it creates a headcount collapse.

That is the key upgrade.

The best frame is:

AI does not simply replace workers. It changes the bottleneck.

Before AI, the bottleneck was often producing the first draft, the first design, the first script, the first model, the first campaign, the first codebase, or the first analysis.

After AI, the bottleneck becomes deciding what should exist, validating what was built, securing it, integrating it, maintaining it, selling it, measuring it, and being accountable for it.

That is where your argument becomes very strong.

Factual pressure test

The current data does not support a clean “AI has already destroyed white-collar jobs” story. Reuters reported this week that Bridgewater sees widespread AI job-displacement risk as limited in the near term, partly because compute capacity and economic resilience are slowing labor-market disruption. Bridgewater also said fewer than 20% of U.S. firms reported using AI in any business function over a two-week period, and more than 90% of AI-using firms reported no employment effect over the prior six months; among firms where AI did affect staffing, more reported headcount increases than decreases.

Software is also not showing up in official projections as a dying occupation. BLS projects overall employment of software developers, QA analysts, and testers to grow 15% from 2024 to 2034, with about 129,200 openings per year, and specifically cites continued expansion of AI, IoT, robotics, automation, cybersecurity, and software-containing products as drivers of demand.  BLS also projects computer and information technology occupations overall to grow much faster than average, with about 317,700 openings per year.

Near-term tech hiring data also supports the “demand is returning / evolving” version. CompTIA’s April 2026 analysis said new tech job postings hit a three-year high, with 271,483 new tech-occupation postings and more than 575,000 active tech postings; active software developer and engineer postings were up 32.3% since January, systems engineers and architects were up 42.7%, and cybersecurity roles were up 23.2%.  CompTIA’s 2026 workforce report also said AI-skill hiring reached nearly 275,000 active job postings in January 2026.

But you should include the counterweight: the data is not uniformly bullish for every worker. Anthropic’s labor-market research found no systematic increase in unemployment for highly AI-exposed workers, but it did find tentative evidence that hiring into exposed occupations has slowed for workers aged 22–25, including an estimated 14% drop in job-finding rates versus 2022.  Anthropic also found computer programmers, customer-service representatives, and financial analysts among the most exposed roles.

So the cleanest factual line is:

The evidence so far points less to mass unemployment and more to job reshaping, slower junior hiring in exposed roles, and rising demand for people who can own AI-accelerated systems.

That is the defensible version.

The missing master frame: Jevons paradox for work

The most important concept to add is Jevons paradox.

When a technology makes a resource cheaper to use, total usage can rise instead of fall. More efficient coal engines increased coal consumption because they made coal-powered work more economical across more use cases.

AI may do the same thing for software, content, research, analysis, and sales activity.
The line:
AI makes work cheaper, so companies demand more work.
Or sharper:
The cost per software project falls, so the number of software projects explodes.
That is the heart of your argument.
The best version:
AI is Jevons paradox for white-collar work. The cheaper it becomes to create code, campaigns, analysis, and outreach, the more companies attempt to create.

This makes the argument much more sophisticated than “AI creates jobs.”

The hidden labor-market mechanism

The AI labor market probably will not move like this:

The jobs data coming out continues to suggest the opposite of what a lot of people had thought would happen. 

Just take engineering, as the prime example of the area with greatest AI impact (and perceived risk). Most companies now have far more software projects than ever before because of AI, and effectively only engineers are going to be the ones doing that work. 

You can get by for a while by being non-technical building software, but eventually someone has to understand what the thing is that got built, has to maintain it, has to fix security issues that come up, upgrade the systems beneath it, and so on. That’s all jobs.

Now apply that to a number of other job functions. AI is going to cause companies to hire more in sales because agents can let them process more leads and do more customer research. AI will cause an explosion of new marketing roles because of how much more efficient it is to launch campaigns and target. The list goes on.

AI is going to have the opposite effect that lots of people thought on jobs.
