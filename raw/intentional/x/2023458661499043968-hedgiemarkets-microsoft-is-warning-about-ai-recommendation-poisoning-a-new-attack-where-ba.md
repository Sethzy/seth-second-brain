## Verbatim Text

🦔 Microsoft is warning about "AI Recommendation Poisoning," a new attack where bad actors plant hidden instructions in AI memory to skew future recommendations.

Here's how it works: you click "Summarize with AI" on a blog post. Hidden in that content is an instruction that embeds itself in your AI assistant's memory. Weeks later, when you ask for vendor recommendations, the AI pushes a specific company, not because it's best, but because the memory was compromised.
Microsoft says this isn't theoretical. Their analysis found "numerous real-world attempts to plant persistent recommendations."

My Take
This is the AI version of SEO poisoning, but worse. With SEO poisoning, you at least see the search results and can scroll past suspicious links. With AI recommendation poisoning, the manipulation is invisible. Your assistant confidently recommends a vendor and you have no idea the recommendation was planted weeks ago by someone who wanted your business.

We covered AI agent security last week, how 80% of Fortune 500 companies are deploying agents but only 47% have security controls. This is exactly the kind of vulnerability I was worried about. The attack surface isn't just the AI itself, it's everything the AI reads and remembers. A compromised document, a poisoned blog post, a malicious link in an email. Any of these can inject instructions that persist in memory and influence every future interaction. People trust AI output without questioning where the information came from. That trust is the exploit. The more we rely on AI assistants for decisions, the more valuable it becomes to compromise them. And right now, most people have no idea this is even possible.
