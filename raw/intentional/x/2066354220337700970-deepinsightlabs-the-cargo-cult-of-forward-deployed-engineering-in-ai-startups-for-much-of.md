# The Cargo Cult of Forward-Deployed Engineering in AI Startups

For much of the past decade, software founders were taught to avoid services. Services depressed gross margins, complicated delivery, made growth dependent on headcount, and obscured whether customers truly wanted the product. The ideal was a product that could be sold repeatedly, deployed predictably, and used without its creators standing beside it.

Artificial intelligence appears to be reversing that doctrine.

Across the industry, startups are hiring forward-deployed engineers, embedding technical teams inside customer organizations, running bespoke pilots, and describing themselves as partners in “transformation” rather than vendors of software. OpenAI has made forward-deployed engineering a central part of its enterprise strategy. Anthropic is building an Applied AI organization while training partners to deploy Claude. Google Cloud, despite already possessing a large enterprise sales and consulting apparatus, is also sending forward-deployed engineers into strategic accounts.

The standard explanation is that AI is different. The technology is powerful but difficult to implement. Enterprises need help integrating their data, redesigning workflows, constructing evaluations, and putting appropriate controls around probabilistic systems. Forward-deployed engineers bridge the gap between a general model and a useful production system.

All of this is true.

But it is not the whole truth.

The sudden enthusiasm for forward-deployed engineering also reflects a more uncomfortable reality: many AI startups have discovered that impressive model capabilities do not naturally assemble themselves into repeatable products. Their demonstrations are compelling, but their products do not consistently deliver value without intensive intervention from the people who built them.

In some companies, forward deployment is a disciplined strategy for discovering a product and becoming embedded in strategically important accounts. In others, it is a flattering label for custom development performed because the product itself has not found a repeatable market.

The distinction matters. Both companies may announce prestigious customers. Both may report annual recurring revenue. Both may employ talented engineers and show impressive demonstrations. But only one is accumulating software economics. The other is accumulating projects.

The danger is that founders and investors increasingly treat Palantir’s forward-deployed engineering model as a template rather than the product of a highly unusual historical context. They copy the visible practice—sending engineers to customers—without reproducing the conditions that made the practice economically viable.

This is cargo-cult management: imitating the rituals associated with success while misunderstanding the system that produced it.

## What Palantir actually proved

Palantir is the reference point for nearly every contemporary discussion of forward-deployed engineering. Its engineers worked alongside intelligence agencies, military units, and large enterprises to integrate fragmented data and build operational systems around difficult, consequential decisions.

But the lesson commonly drawn from Palantir is incomplete.

The superficial lesson is:

Embed excellent engineers with customers, solve important problems, and a valuable platform will emerge.

The more accurate lesson is:

Pursue customers whose contract size, mission criticality, expansion potential, and expected duration can finance expensive deployment work—and then relentlessly convert that work into reusable infrastructure.

Palantir’s early government and defense market was unusually well suited to this approach. Its customers faced heterogeneous legacy systems, classified environments, intricate permission structures, and operational problems for which standard SaaS products were inadequate. The potential value of a successful deployment was enormous, and the cost of switching away from a deeply embedded operational system could be substantial.

The account economics reflected this. In 2019, Palantir reported average revenue of approximately $5.6 million per customer. Its 20 largest customers generated an average of roughly $24.8 million each and had been customers for an average of 6.6 years. Those economics can support expensive technical teams in a way that a $100,000 annual software contract cannot.

Equally important, Palantir invested in making deployment progressively less labor-intensive. It built common capabilities for data integration, permissions, ontology management, applications, and software delivery across constrained environments. The company subsequently reported that its platform investments had materially reduced the number of engineers and the time required to install and deploy its software.

Government and defense gave Palantir the contract economics to fund forward deployment. Productization gave it software economics.

Many startups attempting to copy Palantir possess neither.

They sell smaller contracts into less durable relationships. Their customers are willing to try an AI pilot but not restructure critical operations around it. Their deployments vary widely across industries. Their customer-specific code does not readily become part of a common platform. Yet they adopt the same organizational vocabulary and assume the economics will follow.

They will not.

## Why the model is flourishing now

The current FDE boom has several causes, some constructive and some less so.

The first is that AI has achieved something like capability-market fit before achieving reliable product-market fit.

Enterprises broadly believe that generative AI can create value. Models can write code, analyze documents, interact with software, synthesize evidence, and perform tasks that previously required human judgment. But “this model can perform useful work” is not the same proposition as “this product solves a recurring problem reliably enough to purchase, deploy, and operate at scale.”

The missing work includes:

- identifying the right workflow;

- connecting proprietary data and tools;

- defining acceptable error rates;

- creating evaluation systems;

- allocating responsibility between humans and machines;

- handling exceptions;

- obtaining security and compliance approval;

- changing the surrounding operating process.

Natural-language interfaces made prototypes easier. They did not eliminate enterprise implementation.

The second cause is competitive. Model providers and application startups recognize that the party that defines the initial deployment may acquire a lasting advantage. The first vendor to connect enterprise data, construct agent interfaces, codify evaluation criteria, and redesign a workflow may become deeply embedded even if competing models later reach similar performance.

Forward deployment is therefore not merely customer service. It is a strategy for front-running the market.

A vendor that provides only a model API risks becoming an interchangeable component behind a cloud platform, systems integrator, application vendor, or internal engineering team. A vendor that helps redesign an important business process has a better chance of owning the customer relationship, shaping the architecture, and capturing a larger share of the resulting value.

This helps explain why OpenAI and Anthropic are investing heavily in deployment. OpenAI describes its FDE organization as operating between customer delivery and core platform development and has launched a dedicated deployment company to embed engineers into organizations facing complex problems. Anthropic is pursuing a hybrid strategy: maintaining internal Applied AI expertise while building a partner network and training consulting firms to deliver Claude-based systems.

Google’s behavior is also instructive. It initially appeared less dependent on the FDE narrative because it already possessed Google Cloud, Workspace, enterprise account teams, consulting capabilities, and a global partner ecosystem. Those assets provide distribution that standalone model laboratories must construct themselves.

Yet Google is now deploying FDEs alongside major consulting partners as well. Distribution lowers the cost of reaching enterprises. It does not eliminate the difficulty of translating a general model into a production workflow.

The FDE boom is therefore both a warning and a land grab.

It is a warning that AI products are less complete than their demonstrations imply. It is a land grab because the deployment layer may determine which vendors become entrenched before the product categories fully stabilize.

## When FDE conceals a lack of product-market fit

Product-market fit is often described through observable outcomes: customers buy, remain, expand, and recommend the product. But a sufficiently determined startup can manufacture some of those signals through services.

Suppose a customer will pay $500,000 for a team of engineers to solve a valuable internal problem. The startup may record the contract as recurring software revenue, even though the customer is economically purchasing a hybrid of software, implementation, and technical labor.

The revenue is real. The customer’s problem may be real. The engagement may even be profitable.

But it does not necessarily establish product-market fit for a scalable software product.

Forward deployment can hide three weaknesses.

1. The product does not have a stable unit of value

A company with product-market fit can usually explain what it repeatedly sells. It may sell seats, transactions, workflows, data volume, resolved cases, or access to a defined system.

A company without a stable product often sells an aspiration: “AI transformation,” “enterprise agents,” or “automation of knowledge work.” Its engineers must determine what the product means separately for each account.

The broader the promise, the more likely the FDE team is performing paid product discovery rather than deploying an existing product.

Paid product discovery is not inherently bad. But founders should call it what it is.

2. Each customer requires a different invention

Customer variation is inevitable. The relevant question is whether that variation is handled through configuration or through new engineering.

Healthy variation involves mapping data, selecting connectors, configuring policies, or adapting a reusable workflow. Unhealthy variation requires a new application architecture, new business logic, and a permanent branch of customer-specific code.

The former can become software. The latter scales like a consultancy.

3. Human effort is creating the outcome attributed to the product

In some AI deployments, the apparent intelligence of the system partly reflects constant intervention by engineers: repairing prompts, adjusting retrieval, inspecting outputs, handling edge cases, and manually shepherding workflows.

The customer may experience a successful pilot without acquiring an autonomous or maintainable system.

This is the AI equivalent of a “Wizard of Oz” product continuing long after the experiment should have ended.

The decisive test is simple:

Does the customer continue receiving comparable value after the deployment team withdraws?

When the answer is no, the startup has found demand for its people, not necessarily for its product.

## When forward deployment is strategically rational

The contrarian argument should not become an equally simplistic rejection of services. History shows that substantial customer-facing work can help produce excellent software companies.

ServiceNow is an especially useful precedent. In its early years, its professional-services gross margins were deeply negative—approximately negative 220% in 2009, negative 202% in 2010, and negative 21% in 2011. It nevertheless used implementation work to drive adoption of a reusable workflow platform. Over time, ServiceNow standardized more of the underlying capabilities and relied increasingly on implementation partners.

The company did not need to make services a wonderful business. It needed services to become small relative to a highly attractive subscription business.

Veeva offers a related vertical-software example. Life-sciences implementations still require configuration, data services, training, and business consulting. But the recurring problems are bounded by a common industry, regulatory environment, and set of workflows. In late 2025, Veeva reported subscription gross margins far above the margins on its professional services. The software engine remained economically dominant even though human implementation had not disappeared.

Snowflake goes further: its professional-services margins have at times been materially negative, but those services represent a small share of revenue and help activate much larger product consumption.

The pattern is repeatable, but it is not the pattern most founders imagine.

The historically repeatable path is not:

Hire FDEs, solve bespoke customer problems, and eventually become Palantir.

It is:

Use internal technical services to identify recurring problems; encode those problems into configurable software; narrow the domain; and transfer routine implementation to customers or partners.

Internal delivery first. Product standardization second. Partner delivery third.

Palantir is unusual because it retained a particularly visible and strategically important internal FDE culture while achieving scale. Most successful enterprise-software companies have progressively externalized routine deployment.

## The economic question founders avoid

Forward deployment changes both gross margin and customer acquisition cost.

An engineer who helps close a prospect, builds the pilot, integrates the customer’s systems, and remains involved after launch may be distributed across sales, research and development, and cost of revenue in the company’s accounts.

Economically, however, the allocation is straightforward:

- work required to win the customer is acquisition cost;

- work required to deliver the contracted outcome is cost of revenue;

- work that creates a capability reusable across customers is product development.

Startups frequently classify customer-specific engineering as R&D because engineers report into the product organization. This can make both CAC and gross margin appear better than the underlying economics.

Founders should calculate a fully loaded cost to production:

sales and marketing expense
plus presales engineering
plus unpaid pilot work
plus onboarding and deployment cost
divided by customers that reach durable production use.

They should also calculate contribution margin after customer-specific technical labor.

These measurements may be uncomfortable. That is precisely why they matter.

An FDE model can support excellent economics when account values are large, customers remain for years, and one deployment creates substantial expansion. A $300,000 engineer supporting several million dollars of durable annual revenue may be a good investment. The same engineer effectively dedicated to one $250,000 account may consume almost all of the account’s gross profit before cloud infrastructure and corporate overhead are considered.

The prestige of the customer does not change the arithmetic.

## The five tests founders should apply

Before building an FDE organization, founders should answer five questions.

1. Is the market funding product discovery—or merely renting engineers?

A large customer’s willingness to pay is not enough. The engagement should reveal a problem likely to recur across a meaningful market.

Founders should write down, before accepting an engagement, which parts they expect to reuse and what evidence would falsify that expectation.

2. Will the account economics support the deployment?

The relevant variables are annual contract value, deployment duration, engineers per account, expansion potential, and expected customer lifetime.

An FDE motion attached to seven-figure strategic accounts is structurally different from one attached to mid-five-figure SaaS contracts.

Do not copy the staffing model of a vendor whose accounts are 20 times larger than yours.

3. Is customer variation becoming configuration?

Every deployment should produce reusable components: connectors, schemas, evaluation templates, approval mechanisms, policy controls, or workflow primitives.

The goal is not zero customization. The goal is to move work through a progression:

bespoke development → reusable extension → configuration.

If the tenth customer in a vertical requires roughly the same engineering effort as the first, the organization is learning how to deliver projects, not how to build a product.

4. Can the FDE eventually leave?

A successful deployment creates customer capability. Internal users can operate the system, routine changes do not require the vendor’s engineers, and expansion does not demand proportional additional staffing.

An FDE who can never leave is not an implementation resource. The FDE is part of the service being sold.

5. Is there a credible path to externalizing routine delivery?

Partners are not merely a distribution tactic. They are often the mechanism through which enterprise-software companies decouple growth from internal services headcount.

The durable division of labor is usually:

- internal experts handle new patterns, strategic accounts, and the hardest technical problems;

- partners perform repeatable implementation;

- customers handle ordinary configuration;

- the product absorbs everything that recurs.

A startup with no plan for this transition should be valued and managed as a technology-enabled services business until proven otherwise.

## A practical scorecard

Founders do not need to eliminate FDEs. They need evidence that the model is compounding.

The following metrics are more informative than the number of enterprise logos:

- deployment time by customer cohort;

- fully loaded acquisition and activation cost;

- percentage of pilots reaching production;

- contribution margin after customer-specific engineering;

- annual recurring revenue per FDE;

- accounts supported per FDE;

- expansion revenue per incremental deployment hour;

- percentage of implementation components reused;

- customer-specific code as a share of the maintained codebase;

- time required before the customer can operate without the FDE.

The most revealing question is:

Is the next dollar of revenue becoming less dependent on human delivery?

If the answer remains no after multiple deployments in the same market, founders should stop pretending that productization is imminent.

A services business can still be valuable. It can be profitable, strategically important, and highly defensible. But it should have the pricing, staffing model, financing plan, and valuation expectations appropriate to services.

## Is FDE a fad?

The label is a fad. The function is not.

Complex technologies have always required people who can connect technical capability to customer operations. Enterprise software had implementation consultants. Cloud vendors had solution architects and professional-services teams. Data platforms had resident architects. Industrial companies had application engineers.

AI makes this function more important because the product boundary is unusually fluid. Models are general-purpose, workflows are organization-specific, reliability must be established empirically, and implementation often requires simultaneous technical and organizational redesign.

Forward deployment will therefore persist.

What will fade is the belief that merely hiring FDEs constitutes a strategy.

Over the next several years, the market will probably divide into four groups.

The first will use FDEs temporarily to discover a narrow, repeatable product. These companies may become strong software businesses.

The second will retain FDEs for strategic accounts while partners and customers handle standardized deployments. This will resemble the mature enterprise-platform model.

The third will operate permanent, technology-enabled services businesses. Some will be attractive, but their margins and scaling profiles will differ from SaaS.

The fourth will discover that expensive engineers cannot indefinitely compensate for ambiguous demand, small contracts, and non-reusable implementations. Many of these companies will stall once capital becomes more expensive or early flagship customers fail to expand.

For most startups, the Palantir model will not work as imagined. Palantir combined unusually large and durable accounts, mission-critical problems, deep technical embeddedness, substantial expansion, and years of platform investment. Copying its organizational form without those conditions is unlikely to reproduce its results.

## The founder’s real choice

The relevant decision is not whether to be “product-led” or “FDE-led.” That framing is too crude.

The real choice is what role human effort will play in the company’s learning system.

Human deployment can be used to manufacture the appearance of product-market fit. Engineers can rescue weak products, satisfy individual customers, and make every pilot look successful. This produces revenue but little leverage.

Or human deployment can be used to accelerate the discovery of a repeatable system. Engineers can uncover recurrent workflows, identify missing primitives, develop reference architectures, and make each subsequent deployment easier.

The activities may look identical from outside. The difference appears in the direction of the economics.

Healthy FDE organizations experience declining deployment effort, rising reuse, greater revenue per engineer, expanding customer autonomy, and a growing share of value delivered by the product.

Unhealthy ones experience larger delivery teams, proliferating exceptions, permanent customer-specific code, flattering but misleading ARR, and a roadmap increasingly controlled by whichever account shouts loudest.

Founders should be especially suspicious when “being close to the customer” becomes an argument against narrowing the product. Customer intimacy is useful only when it enables abstraction. Otherwise, it becomes servitude.

## Beyond the cargo cult

Forward-deployed engineering is neither a cheat code nor an admission of failure. It is a costly instrument.

Used well, it can help a startup co-invent a category, win strategic accounts before competitors, discover the true product, and create deep organizational switching costs. Used badly, it can allow a company to postpone the moment when it must prove that a product exists independently of the people delivering it.

The difference is not whether engineers sit with customers.

It is whether the company becomes progressively less dependent on doing so.

Palantir’s lasting lesson is not that startups should deploy engineers forward. It is that expensive customer work must create an asset that compounds: a platform, an architecture, a domain model, a distribution advantage, or an expanding base of reusable intellectual property.

Without that compounding asset, FDE is simply consulting in a hoodie.

And consulting is not a bad business. It is merely a different one.
