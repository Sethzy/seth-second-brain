## Core Thesis

Enterprise outbound is not mainly a volume game unless the addressable market is huge. If the account pool is small, careless outreach burns the territory. The job is to patiently find the highest-signal reason to speak to each person, then reach out when the context makes the conversation feel obvious.

The operating question:

> When is this prospect most likely to want to talk to me?

Everything else is subordinate to that question: research, timing, warm paths, events, proof points, message tone, and cadence.

## When This Applies

Use this playbook when the market has one or more of these traits:

- Small or finite account universe.
- Enterprise or mid-market ACVs where one conversation can matter.
- Strategic accounts where multiple stakeholders need to be mapped.
- Technical products where credibility matters more than generic persuasion.
- AI, fintech, workflow, developer-tooling, or enterprise-transformation categories where the buyer is anxious, skeptical, or overloaded by hype.
- New-region GTM where reputation, referrals, and local market context compound over time.

Do not use this playbook for huge-market SMB blasting, where low reply rates are acceptable and TAM is effectively disposable.

## The Anti-Pattern

The weak motion:

- Build a list.
- Connect with everyone.
- Send templated cold messages.
- Follow up aggressively.
- Expect the same generic message to work across all accounts.

This can work statistically only when the total lead universe is very large. In an enterprise territory, it creates a worse problem: every weak touch puts the lead on cooldown and reduces the chance of a better future conversation.

Interview phrasing:

> I think about enterprise outbound as territory stewardship. In SMB you can sometimes survive on volume. In enterprise, especially with a narrow ICP, every low-context touch spends trust. I would rather wait for a stronger reason to engage than burn a strategic account with a generic message.

## The High-Signal Sales Model

The goal is to build a research and relationship flywheel:

1. Start with the best available warm or contextual entry points.
2. Have a few high-quality conversations each week.
3. Extract useful market hints, names, objections, and account context from each conversation.
4. Use those hints to make the next outreach more relevant.
5. Let repetition create a local reputation before the next conversation even starts.

The practical target is not "message 100 people." It is "find the best 3 people to reach out to today."

Operating line:

> Always run a cron job that surfaces the best three people to reach out to every day.

## Signal Waterfall

Use this order when deciding whether someone is ready for direct outreach.

### 1. Warm Intro Or Trusted Referral

Highest-quality signal. A trusted person says the prospect is relevant, opinionated, knowledgeable, or currently dealing with the problem.

Best pattern:

> I spoke with [mutual contact], and they said you were the right person to ask about [specific workflow/problem]. I just joined/work on [company/category], and I am trying to understand whether [problem] shows up similarly at [their company]. Open to a quick chat?

Why it works:

- The ask is socially grounded.
- The message makes the prospect feel selected, not targeted.
- It gives them status as someone knowledgeable.
- It opens a discovery conversation without hard-selling.

### 2. Alumni, School, Geography, Or Shared Tribe

Use common ground first, especially in markets where trust is relational.

Examples:

- Same university or overseas education path.
- Same region or expat community.
- Same professional ecosystem.
- Same founder/operator background.
- Same technical or industry pain.

Seth's field note: in San Francisco, alumni connection was often the only thing that worked. For legal/Sunder conversations, Cambridge/law adjacency helped make the ask credible.

Interview phrasing:

> I do not treat alumni or shared background as a gimmick. It is a trust bridge. If I can make the first 30 seconds feel human and relevant, the buyer is much more willing to tell me how the world actually works.

### 3. Prior Company Or Workflow Relevance

Look at work history and find lived operational overlap.

Examples:

- Former fintech operator talking to another fintech operator about bank APIs, payment rails, compliance, or onboarding friction.
- Engineering/product leader who has lived bad internal tooling or integration pain.
- Revenue leader who has been burned by bad CRM hygiene, poor enrichment, or low-context outbound.

The message should not say "I saw you worked at X" in a mechanical way. It should use that fact to ask a better question.

Pattern:

> I have been thinking about how [workflow pain] shows up in [industry/company type]. My experience at [relevant context] was that [specific friction] became the real blocker. Curious if that maps to what you have seen at [their company/current market].

### 4. Competitor Or Peer-Company Intelligence

If a competitor, peer, customer, or market insider gives a hint, use it carefully.

Examples:

- "I heard teams like yours are wrestling with [specific problem]."
- "Someone in the market mentioned [workflow] is surprisingly painful in [region/segment]."
- "We have been speaking with teams similar to [peer company] and are seeing [pattern]."

Do not overplay confidential information. The point is to create curiosity and invite correction.

Why it works:

- People are curious about competitors.
- Opinionated operators often want to correct an outsider's half-formed view.
- The conversation starts around the market, not the product.

### 5. Product Or Category Intent Signals

Track whether the prospect engages with relevant category content.

Signals:

- Follows the company's page.
- Likes or comments on company posts.
- Interacts with competitor or adjacent category pages.
- Posts about AI adoption, tooling, developer productivity, automation, compliance, cost pressure, or transformation.
- Uses language that suggests they are experimenting but unsure what to do next.

For AI GTM, relevant signals may include engagement with OpenAI, Anthropic/Claude, Cursor, Codex, developer tooling, AI adoption, AI governance, or workflow automation content.

Message principle:

- Do not say "I saw you liked our post" if it feels creepy or automated.
- Use the topic as the entry point.

Pattern:

> I have been thinking about [topic from their interaction/post]. One thing we are seeing is [sharp point of view]. Curious how you are thinking about it in [their environment].

### 6. Event Hijacking

Events create timely relevance.

Use:

- Prospect is attending an event.
- Prospect posted after attending an event.
- You are in town.
- Your company is hosting or sponsoring something.
- A relevant speaker said something provocative.
- The event agenda reveals what the market is being primed to care about.

Patterns:

> Saw you were at [event]. The bit that stood out to me was [specific speaker/topic]. I am curious whether that resonated with teams actually trying to deploy this inside [company/industry].

> I am in [city] next week speaking with a few [role/company type] teams about [problem]. If useful, happy to compare notes while I am in town.

Psychology:

- Events create recency.
- People are more willing to discuss a topic they just heard framed.
- In-person availability gives the outreach a concrete reason to exist.
- A senior enterprise seller can use "I am in town" as a legitimate forcing function.

### 7. Cold Only For The Super-Hot

Pure cold should be reserved for people with unusually high fit or urgency. Everyone else goes into a drip/monitoring pool until a stronger signal appears.

Rule:

> Only target the super-hot ones for pure cold. Leave the rest for drip.

## Research And Enrichment Loop

Seth's AI-native version of enterprise prospecting is a daily/weekly research loop, not manual clicking.

Data inputs:

- Account list.
- People list.
- LinkedIn profiles and public posts.
- Company pages and product pages.
- Company news.
- Hiring signals.
- Event attendance and speaker agendas.
- Social interactions with relevant category content.
- Existing CRM notes.
- Internal Slack/sales channels, where available.
- Prior conversations and referrals.

Enrichment columns:

- Account fit.
- Persona fit.
- Seniority and likely budget influence.
- Work-history overlap.
- Alumni/shared background.
- Category intent signals.
- Recent event signal.
- Recent post/comment signal.
- Relevant peer/customer proof point.
- Suggested hook.
- Reach-out readiness.
- Cooldown status.
- Next best action.

Daily output:

- Top 3 prospects to contact today.
- Top 10 to monitor.
- People to leave alone until a better signal appears.

Weekly output:

- Accounts with fresh signals.
- New warm-intro paths.
- Events worth hijacking.
- Message themes that are starting to repeat.
- Objections or market anxieties that should inform content and proof points.

Interview phrasing:

> I would build the territory almost like an intelligence system. The automation is not there to spam people. It is there to tell the seller where patience is over and timing is finally right.

## Channel Strategy

### LinkedIn

Use LinkedIn when:

- The context is social, alumni, event, or thought-leadership driven.
- The prospect is active on the platform.
- The message benefits from a lightweight, conversational tone.
- You want to connect them to company content before asking for time.

Suggested sequence:

1. Connect.
2. Invite/funnel them toward following the company page if appropriate.
3. Let relevant marketing/category content drip.
4. Watch for interaction or topic signal.
5. Reach out when the hook is stronger.

### Email

Use email when:

- LinkedIn automation or rate limits are risky.
- The domain has strong credibility.
- You can write a clean, specific message.
- You need better automation and tracking.

Seth's note: if the sender domain is highly trusted and the motion is not spammy, email can be a safer automation path.

### Events And In-Person

Use events when:

- The territory is geographically concentrated.
- Buyers are attending the same AI/business/industry events.
- You need market conversations faster than cold outbound can produce.
- You can use "I am in town" or "I was also at [event]" as a natural reason.

This may outperform LinkedIn for early-region enterprise GTM because it compresses trust.

## Cadence Rules

- Slow down.
- Do not bump too often.
- Treat each outreach as spending the lead's attention.
- Once contacted, the lead goes on cooldown.
- Wait for new context before re-engaging.
- Prefer 3 strong conversations per week over 100 weak touches.

Operating line:

> If you can get three good conversations a week, it starts snowballing.

## Proof-Point Matching

Generic logos do not create FOMO. The proof point must feel close to the prospect's world.

Bad:

- "We work with impressive company X" when X is unrelated to the buyer.
- "We helped a tech company" when the buyer needs evidence from their workflow, region, risk model, or persona.

Good:

- For travel, use travel proof.
- For fintech, use payment/API/compliance proof.
- For AI adoption, use teams actually deploying AI in similar workflows.
- For legal/professional services, use document operations and human-review proof.
- For enterprise transformation, use stakeholder mapping, pilot scoping, procurement, risk removal, and adoption proof.

Interview phrasing:

> Enterprise proof has to be proximate. A Malaysian bank is not going to care about a flashy AI logo if the use case feels culturally and operationally distant. The question is: what story makes this buyer think, "that is basically us"?

## AI Buyer Psychology

Many enterprise buyers, especially technical or engineering-heavy teams, are anxious or skeptical about AI.

Common buyer beliefs:

- "AI is overhyped."
- "My team is better than this."
- "I do not trust the output."
- "I will need to check everything anyway."
- "This will create more risk than productivity."
- "We do not know where to start."

Sales implication:

- Move slowly.
- Sell reviewable workflows, not magic.
- Emphasize human-in-the-loop, validation, auditability, and source-backed outputs.
- Respect the buyer's fear instead of mocking it.
- Show the smallest credible workflow where AI reduces toil without removing expert judgment.

This links directly to Seth's broader positioning: workflow judgment becoming software.

## Message Patterns

### Warm Referral

> [Name] mentioned you were one of the sharper people to ask about [specific workflow]. I am trying to understand how [problem] is showing up inside [company/segment], especially as teams start testing [category]. Open to a quick chat next week?

### Shared Background

> I noticed we both came through [school/market/background]. I am speaking with [persona] teams about [specific workflow], and your path through [relevant company/industry] made me think you would have a useful view. Would love to compare notes.

### Work-History Relevance

> I saw you spent time around [industry/workflow]. My experience was that [specific operational pain] was the real blocker, not the obvious surface problem. Curious if that has been true in your current role as well.

### Competitor Or Peer Curiosity

> We have been hearing from teams around [peer segment] that [problem] is becoming a real bottleneck. I am not sure if that maps to [their company], but I would value your read.

### Event Follow-Up

> I saw you were at [event]. The discussion around [speaker/topic] felt very relevant to [their function]. I am curious whether that is just conference talk or something teams like yours are actually acting on.

### Category Intent

> I have been thinking a lot about [topic]. One pattern I keep seeing is that companies want the AI productivity upside, but the real blocker is trust, review, and workflow fit. Is that how you are seeing it?

### In-Town

> I am in [city] next week meeting a few [persona/company type] teams about [problem]. If useful, happy to share what I am seeing and hear how it compares to [their company].

### 30-Second Pitch

> My enterprise sales style is high-signal and research-led. I do not believe in burning a narrow territory with generic outbound. I map the account, find the strongest human or business reason to talk, wait for the right trigger, and use each conversation to compound into the next. That fits my background: I have sold enterprise fintech and partnership workflows, built AI GTM systems, and learned that the best enterprise motion is not more activity. It is better timing, better relevance, and stronger proof.

### 2-Minute STAR

Situation:

> In enterprise GTM, especially for AI or technical products, the buyer pool is finite and skeptical. A generic sequence can create meetings, but it also burns the territory and produces low-quality conversations.

Task:

> My approach is to create a motion that surfaces the few people worth contacting now, with a reason that is specific enough to feel credible.

Action:

> I map accounts and people, enrich them with work-history, alumni, event, competitor, category-intent, and warm-intro signals, then prioritize the top few prospects each day. I tailor proof points so they feel proximate to the buyer's world, and I keep cadence slow enough that I do not waste trust. The automation does the research and prioritization; the actual relationship-building stays human.

Result:

> That is the same logic behind my Salescraft work: TAM mapping, account enrichment, fit scoring, signal-based outbound, and seller handoffs. It is also how I think about founder-led discovery from Sunder: use trust wedges, ask real workflow questions, earn referrals, and let each conversation improve the next one.

### Likely Follow-Ups

Q: How do you balance patience with quota pressure?

> I separate activity for learning from activity for conversion. In a new territory I still run fast experiments, attend events, ask for intros, and create content, but I avoid generic outreach to strategic accounts until I have a real hook. The goal is not to be passive; it is to make every direct touch count.

Q: Is this scalable?

> Yes, but the scaled part is research and prioritization, not robotic sending. I would automate signal collection, enrichment, scoring, and daily recommendations. The final message and relationship decision should stay human for high-value accounts.

Q: What would you build first in a new enterprise territory?

> A living account intelligence table: accounts, buying committees, warm paths, category signals, event signals, proof-point matches, current hypothesis, next best action, and cooldown. Then a weekly rhythm where the system surfaces the few people worth contacting and the reason why.

Q: How do you avoid sounding creepy with social signals?

> Use the topic, not the surveillance. If someone engages with AI governance content, I do not say "I saw you liked this post." I start a normal conversation about the topic and ask for their view.

Q: What is your view on AI in enterprise sales?

> AI should make sellers more thoughtful, not noisier. The best use is to read the market, find timing, match proof, and prepare sharper conversations. If it just helps you send more generic messages, it makes enterprise sales worse.

## EU / EMEA Adaptation

For enterprise EU or EMEA roles, emphasize:

- Smaller-market territory discipline.
- Multi-country localization rather than one-size-fits-all messaging.
- Slower trust-building in regulated or relationship-heavy markets.
- Sensitivity to local buyer norms, procurement, legal, and compliance.
- Event-led and partner-led motion as a complement to cold outbound.
- Proof proximity by country, industry, and operational workflow.
- Ability to translate APAC market-entry experience into another fragmented regional market.

Recruiter phrasing:

> My APAC experience maps well to EU/EMEA because both require localized GTM judgment. You cannot sell the same way across every market. You need to understand local proof, buyer anxiety, partner paths, procurement norms, and the few signals that make outreach feel relevant.
