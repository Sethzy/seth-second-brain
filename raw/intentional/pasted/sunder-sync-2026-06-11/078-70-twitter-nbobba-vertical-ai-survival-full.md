## Full Article (Partial - Article Format)

### Introduction

In the past 3 weeks, Anthropic in particular has been on an absolute tear. Claude Code, Cowork, Claude for Excel and Claude for legal review have left the market asking questions of the entire SaaS industry and emerging Vertical AI ecosystem at large.

[Quotes Jack Altman's tweet: "The current consensus view is saas is dead...presuming that's right, the next interesting next question is What companies are 'safe from ai'?"]

Vertical AI was supposed to be the application-layer opportunity for VC funds raising this cycle. Leverage foundation models, entrench yourself deeply into a vertical, build agents to coordinate hairy longtail workflows and hopefully build a defensible product and business.

But foundation model companies know that verticalization drives diffusion, adoption, and revenue so now they are verticalizing and they're doing it fast.

Should Vertical AI companies be worried? Of course, but not all of them equally.

### The Last Mile of AI

I think the Vertical AI companies that can compete are those that own what I call the **last mile of AI**, delivering intelligence for a specific outcome in a specific context with specific accountability.

This means:
- Coordinating intelligence and outcomes with real world liability, administrative and regulatory endpoints
- Managing relationships with authorities, auditors, and real world systems
- Handling exceptions and edge cases that models cannot anticipate
- Delivering outcomes transcending both the digital and real world

### Last Mile Defensibility is a Spectrum

I think of last mile coordination on a spectrum of short to long. Short is more susceptible to foundation model creep and long is less so. Naturally, the last mile is not equally long in every domain. Some domains are more exposed than others.

#### Shortest Last Mile
Marketing copy, image generation, content drafting. These are transactional output businesses. **Foundation models will absorb them if they haven't already.** See $FIG for reference.

#### Short
Coding assistants, basic legal drafting, research copilot. Useful, but thin. **Claude and OpenAI are already here.**

#### Medium
Contract analysis, financial modeling, internal workflow automation. We are beginning to see Claude, OpenAI and Gemini stake their flags here. **Vertical AI companies such as Rogo, Harvey and Hebbia have wedges that start here but must quickly move into the long last mile if they haven't already.**

#### Long
**Tax compliance with filing, audit-ready accounting, licensed professional services. Cross-border regulatory coordination, healthcare with FDA and HIPAA requirements, anything that requires coordination with real world systems. These have structural defensibility.**

[Quotes Jon Chu (Khosla Ventures): "One clear outcome of the AI trade is that while some SaaS companies get hit hard, operationally heavy ones like $OPEN and $DASH will disproportionately benefit from this."]

## Analysis

**Thesis:** Defensibility correlates directly with "last mile" length - the distance between LLM output and real-world outcome with accountability.

**Foundation Model Threat:** Anthropic's rapid verticalization (Code, Cowork, Excel, Legal) demonstrates that foundation models will absorb short last-mile use cases.

**Spectrum Framework:**
- **Short:** Transactional outputs (marketing, drafting) - already dead
- **Medium:** Analysis and modeling (contracts, financials) - battleground
- **Long:** Real-world coordination with regulation - defensible

**Named Companies:**
- **Rogo, Harvey, Hebbia:** Start in medium, must move to long
- **Figma ($FIG):** Mentioned as reference for short last-mile disruption
- **OpenDoor ($OPEN), DoorDash ($DASH):** Operationally heavy, benefit from AI

**Regulatory Moat:** FDA, HIPAA, cross-border compliance, licensed services create structural defensibility because foundation models cannot file taxes, coordinate audits, or interface with regulatory authorities.

**Real-World Systems:** The key differentiator is coordination with non-digital systems - auditors, authorities, filing systems, licensed professionals.

## Strategic Implications

**VC Thesis Impact:** "Vertical AI was supposed to be the application-layer opportunity" - this thesis is under threat from foundation model verticalization.

**Race to the Long Last Mile:** Vertical AI companies in medium territory (Rogo, Harvey, Hebbia) must quickly add regulatory/real-world coordination or face commoditization.

**Operational Leverage:** Companies like OpenDoor and DoorDash with heavy operational coordination benefit because their moat is real-world complexity, not software sophistication.

**Portfolio Defense:** VCs funding Vertical AI must evaluate: How long is the last mile? Can this company reach regulatory/real-world coordination before foundation models commoditize the core offering?
