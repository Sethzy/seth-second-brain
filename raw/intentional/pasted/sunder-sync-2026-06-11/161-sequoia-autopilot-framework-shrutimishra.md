# Sequoia's Autopilot Framework — The Next $1T Company

**Author:** Shruti (@heyshrutimishra)
**Source:** https://x.com/heyshrutimishra (X/Twitter Article)
**Date:** April 3, 2026
**Views:** 42.5K

---

The next $1T company won't build software.
It'll replace the people using it.

Sequoia just published the framework. Most founders read it and missed the point entirely.

Here's what they actually said:

## The Core Problem

Every founder building an AI tool is asking the same question: what happens when the next version of Claude makes my product a feature?

If you sell the tool, you're in a race against the model.
But if you sell the work, every improvement in the model makes your service faster, cheaper, and harder to compete with.

For every $1 spent on software, businesses spend $6 on services.
That $6 market is where the opportunity lives.

## The Framework: Intelligence vs Judgement

Most work falls somewhere on a spectrum:

**Intelligence work:** translating a spec into code, testing, debugging, filling forms, matching data against rules. The rules are complex but they are rules.

**Judgement work:** deciding which feature to build next, whether to take on tech debt, when to ship before it's ready. It requires experience and taste built on years of practice.

AI has crossed the threshold where it can do most intelligence work autonomously.
Judgement still needs humans.

The higher the intelligence ratio in any field, the sooner autopilots win.

## The 2x2 Matrix That Maps $1T in Opportunities

Sequoia mapped every major service vertical across two dimensions:

- Vertical axis: Intelligence (top) to Judgement (bottom)
- Horizontal axis: Outsourced (right) to Insourced (left)

This produces four quadrants:

### AUTOPILOT TERRITORY (top right)
High intelligence, already outsourced. This is where you start.

Examples:
- Insurance brokerage: $140-200B
- Accounting & audit: $50-80B outsourced
- Healthcare revenue cycle: $50-80B
- Claims adjusting: $50-80B
- Tax advisory: $30-35B
- Legal transactional work: $20-25B

### NEXT WAVE (bottom right)
Lower intelligence requirements, outsourced. Target these as AI improves.

Examples:
- Supply chain & procurement: $200B+
- Pharmacy back-office: $30B+
- Wealth management ops: $30B+

### COPILOT TERRITORY (top left)
High intelligence, but insourced. These stay tools for now.

Examples:
- Management consulting: $300-400B (mostly judgement)
- Graphic design: $30B+
- Executive search: $20B+

### WATCH (bottom left)
Lower judgement, insourced. Harder to displace because budget lives inside the company.

Examples:
- Recruitment: $200B+
- Advertising: $100B+
- Admin assistants: $80B+

## The Autopilot Playbook: Where to Start

The right place to start is where outsourcing already exists.

If a task is already outsourced, it tells you three things:
1. The company has accepted that this work can be done externally
2. There's an existing budget line you can substitute cleanly
3. The buyer is already purchasing an outcome, not a tool

Replacing an outsourcing contract with an AI service = vendor swap.
Replacing headcount = reorg.

Vendor swaps are faster, cleaner, and require less convincing.

### The Playbook:
1. Start with the outsourced, intelligence-heavy task.
2. Nail distribution.
3. Expand toward the insourced, judgement-heavy work as your AI compounds proprietary data.

The outsourced task is the wedge. The insourced work is the long-term TAM.

## Who's Doing It Right (With Examples)

**Crosby (legal):** Started with NDAs. A well-defined task, primarily intelligence, that most companies already outsource to external counsel. The budget exists, the scope is clear, the ROI is immediate.

**WithCoverage (insurance):** Standard commercial lines are highly standardised. The broker's value-add is shopping across carriers and filling forms. Pure intelligence work. The distribution layer is fragmented (tens of thousands of small brokers), so no single incumbent controls the customer relationship.

**Rillet (accounting):** Building the AI-native ERP that closes the books. The US has lost 340,000 accountants over five years while demand has grown. 75% of CPAs are nearing retirement. That structural shortage is pushing firms to accept AI faster than almost any other profession.

**Anterior (healthcare billing):** Medical coding is translating clinical notes into 70,000 standardised ICD-10 codes. The rules are complex but they are rules. The outsourcing is already mature and outcome-based. An autopilot just has to do the same thing at lower cost.

**Pace (claims adjusting):** Standard-line claims are settled by interpreting policy language against damage schedules and setting reserves using actuarial tables. The adjuster workforce is aging out and nobody's replacing them.

## The Hard Truth: Why This Is Difficult

Selling outcomes is fundamentally different from selling software.

When you sell software, you sell a tool. The customer uses the tool. The customer owns the outcome.
When you sell outcomes, accountability shifts to you.

That means:

### 1. Your error tolerance has to be near zero
A bug in a tool is annoying. A wrong outcome on a tax return or insurance claim is a lawsuit.
You need human backstop until your AI earns the right to fly alone.

### 2. Your unit economics look like services, not SaaS
In year 1-3, you're running a services business with better margins.
You're staffing operations people to handle edge cases.
Your gross margins are 50-70%, not 85-90%.

### 3. Trust is the real moat
Incumbents in services businesses don't just have customers. They have relationships built on years of personal accountability.
The broker who handled your claim in 2019. The accountant who called you on a Sunday when something looked wrong.
You have to earn that trust one outcome at a time.

### 4. Distribution is harder than you think
The buyer persona changes completely.
You're not selling to a VP of Engineering who understands AI.
You're selling to a CFO, a COO, a compliance officer who cares about outcomes, not models.

## The Five Mistakes Founders Make

**Mistake 1: Starting with insourced work**
If the task lives inside the company, you're asking them to restructure their team. Start with what they already outsource.

**Mistake 2: Targeting judgement-heavy verticals first**
Management consulting sounds huge ($300B+), but the work is mostly judgement. The intelligence layer isn't large enough yet to wedge in.

**Mistake 3: Underestimating the human backstop required**
Most founders think they can automate 90% on day one. Reality is closer to 60-70% for the first year, with humans handling edge cases.

**Mistake 4: Copying the copilot pricing model**
You're not selling seats. You're selling outcomes. Price per outcome, not per user. Invoice like a service provider, not a SaaS company.

**Mistake 5: Ignoring regulatory moats**
CPA licensing, insurance broker licensing, legal practice rules. These aren't bugs. They're moats. Use them.

## The Tactical Playbook: What to Do First

**Step 1: Pick your vertical from the autopilot quadrant**
High intelligence, already outsourced. Insurance brokerage, accounting, healthcare billing, claims adjusting, tax advisory, legal transactional work, IT managed services.

**Step 2: Find the smallest wedge**
Don't try to replace the entire accounting function. Start with month-end close for companies under $10M revenue. Don't replace all insurance brokerage. Start with standard commercial lines for SMBs.

**Step 3: Build the human-in-the-loop version first**
Your AI handles 70% of the work. Humans review every output before it goes to the customer. Measure your error rate ruthlessly.

**Step 4: Price like a service, not like software**
Per closed book, not per seat. Per drafted contract, not per user. Per filed claim, not per license.

**Step 5: Only sell the outcome you can guarantee**
If you can't guarantee the outcome without human review, you're still a copilot. Don't call yourself an autopilot until you can take full accountability.

**Step 6: Build the data moat**
Every outcome you deliver generates proprietary data on what good looks like in your domain. This is your defensibility. The models are commoditizing. The data compounds.

## The Convergence: Copilot to Autopilot

Today's judgement will become tomorrow's intelligence.

As AI systems accumulate proprietary data about what good judgement looks like in their domain, the frontier shifts.

Harvey started as a copilot for law firms. It's moving quickly to autopilot.
Basis started as a copilot for accountants. The wedge is helping them do the work. The long game is doing the work.

The copilot-to-autopilot transition has already begun in several categories.

But the starting position matters because it determines where you can win customers now and begin compounding the data that will eventually let you handle judgement too.

## The Bottom Line

If you're building an AI tool, ask yourself:

Are you selling the hammer or building the house?

If you're selling the hammer, you're competing with Claude 5, GPT-6, and every model improvement.
If you're building the house, every model improvement makes you faster and cheaper.

The next $1T company won't be a better copilot.
It'll be the first autopilot that earns customer trust at scale.

Pick your vertical. Find the wedge. Build the human backstop. Price the outcome. Compound the data.

That's the playbook.
